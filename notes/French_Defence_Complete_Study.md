## Part 1 — The French in One Page: The Soul of the Opening

The French Defence begins **1.e4 e6**. Black's plan from move one is philosophical and consistent:

> **"I will challenge your centre with ...d5, accept a slightly cramped but rock-solid position, and then break the centre open with a pawn lever (...c5 or ...f6) at a moment of my choosing, generating play against a fixed target."**

Everything else flows from this. If you understand the four structural truths below, you will never be *completely* lost in a French.

### 1.1 The defining feature: the bad bishop

After **1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5**, Black commits to a pawn on e6. This pawn, and later a pawn on d5, sit on **light squares**. Black's **light-squared bishop on c8** is therefore hemmed in by its own pawns. This piece is the eternal problem child of the French — "the French bishop."

**The single most important strategic theme in the entire opening is: what happens to Black's light-squared bishop?**

Black has, broadly, five ways to solve it:

1. **...b6 and ...Ba6** — trade it off by going around the pawn chain (common in Advance/Winawer).
2. **...Bd7–...Bb5 or ...Ba4** — reroute it via d7 (common when White has played a knight to c3 that can be challenged, or to trade light bishops on the queenside).
3. **...f6** break, then ...Bd7-e8-g6/h5 or freeing it after the centre opens.
4. **...cxd4 and ...Bc5/...Bb4** — when an early ...c5 opens lines, sometimes the c8 bishop gets developed after ...Bd7 anyway, but the *dark* bishop becomes the active one.
5. **Live with it** as a defender of light squares (e6, f7) and accept it's passive, relying on the dark-squared bishop and knights for activity.

> **Mental rule for Black:** Every plan you make should pass the test: *"Does this help my light-squared bishop, or at least make its passivity irrelevant?"*
>
> **Mental rule for White:** *"Keep Black's light-squared bishop bad. Trade off Black's GOOD pieces (especially the dark-squared bishop and active knights), and keep the c8-bishop entombed."*

### 1.2 The pawn chains and the golden rule of pawn chains

The French is the textbook home of the **locked pawn chain**. After, say, **1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5**, we get:

- **White's chain:** d4–e5 (pointing toward Black's kingside).
- **Black's chain:** d5–e6 (pointing toward White's queenside).

The classical rule (from Aron Nimzowitsch, who built much of his theory *on* the French) is:

> **Attack a pawn chain at its base.**

- The **base of White's chain is d4** (e5 is the head; d4 is the foundation supporting it). Black attacks d4 with **...c5**, then ...Nc6, ...Qb6, sometimes ...f6 to also hit the head e5. This is why **...c5 is Black's thematic break** in almost every French structure.
- The **base of Black's chain is e6** (d5 is the head). White attacks e6 with **f4–f5** in many lines, or pressures d5. White's thematic pawn lever on the kingside is often **f4–f5**; in the centre White wants to keep d4/e5 solid.

So the whole middlegame is frequently a race:
- **Black plays on the queenside and centre** (...c5, ...Nc6, ...Qb6, ...c4 sometimes, minority-ish pressure on d4).
- **White plays on the kingside** (Nf3, often Bd3, sometimes f4 and a kingside pawn storm / piece attack toward h7).

**Whoever's attack is faster, or whoever's break lands more effectively, usually wins.** Knowing *which side of the board is yours* is half of French strategy.

### 1.3 The two thematic pawn breaks for Black: ...c5 and ...f6

- **...c5** hits d4, the base. Almost always Black's first and most important break. It opens the c-file and pressures the centre. After ...c5 and an eventual ...cxd4, Black often gets the c-file and queenside play.
- **...f6** hits e5, the head, and is used especially when White's e5-pawn is a long-term cramping nuisance (Advance, Classical). It opens the f-file for Black and challenges White's space, but it can also expose Black's own king and the e6-pawn, so timing matters.

> **When to play ...f6:** when you can meet exf6 with a good recapture (...Nxf6 or ...Qxf6 or ...gxf6 reaching a decent structure), when your king is safe enough, and when it genuinely loosens White's grip. Premature ...f6 can hand White the e5-square and open lines toward your king.

### 1.4 Space, the cramped feeling, and why it's okay

White almost always has **more space** in the French (especially when the centre is locked with e5). This is the price Black pays. The compensation is:
- A **concrete target** (the d4–e5 chain, especially the d4 base).
- **Solidity** — Black's position has very few weaknesses and is hard to break down.
- **Counterattacking resources** once the breaks land.

The danger for Black is *passivity* — drifting without a plan, letting White build an overwhelming kingside attack. The danger for White is *over-extension* — pushing pawns, getting the centre dismantled, and being left with weak squares (especially the dark squares around d4 and the e5-square if the e5-pawn falls or is traded).

### 1.5 Piece "jobs" cheat-sheet (applies across most variations)

**For Black:**
- **Light-squared bishop (c8):** the problem piece. Job: get traded, rerouted, or made irrelevant. Until then it defends e6/f7/light squares.
- **Dark-squared bishop (f8):** often Black's *good* bishop. In the Winawer it goes to b4 and frequently trades itself for the c3-knight to wreck White's pawns. In other lines it can go to e7, or to b4/d6/c5 depending on structure. Generally a valuable attacking/defending piece — don't trade it cheaply unless the trade has a point (e.g. damaging White's pawns).
- **King's knight (g8):** goes to f6 (Classical) hitting e4, or to e7 (Winawer/Advance) heading for f5 or g6, or supports ...c5/...f6 breaks. On f5 it eyes d4 and the kingside.
- **Queen's knight (b8):** goes to c6 to hit d4, or to d7 (Tarrasch lines) to support ...c5 and reroute to f6/b6/f8. Knight on c6 pressures d4; knight on d7 is flexible and supports e5/c5 breaks.
- **Queen:** loves **b6** (hitting d4 and b2) and sometimes **a5** (in Winawer, hitting the chain/checking ideas) or **c7**. The queen on b6 is a signature French move adding a second attacker to d4.
- **Rooks:** the **c-file** (after ...c5xd4 or ...cxd4) is Black's highway. An ...f6 break opens the **f-file**. Rooks belong on c8 and f8/e8 depending on which break you commit to.
- **King:** usually castles short. In some Winawer lines Black delays castling or even keeps the king in the centre because White's dark-squared attack makes kingside castling risky; queenside is rare but possible.

**For White:**
- **Light-squared bishop (f1):** White's "good" bishop in many lines — it gets outside the pawn chain. Goes to **d3** (aiming at h7 — the classic French attacking bishop, the "Greek gift" Bxh7+ sacrifice is a constant theme), or **b5**/e2, or stays to support an f5 break. In the Tarrasch and Advance, the d3-bishop pointing at h7 is a key attacking asset.
- **Dark-squared bishop (c1):** can be a problem child for *White* too, especially in the Winawer where White often gives it up or it's blocked by the d4-pawn. Often developed to e3 (defending d4), d2, g5 (pinning ...Nf6 in Classical), or fianchettoed in some KIA-flavoured setups. After e5, the c1-bishop has the c1–h6 diagonal but d4 can be in its way.
- **Knights:** **Nf3** supports d4 and e5 and eyes g5/h4-f5 ideas; **Nc3** hits d5 and e4 (Classical/Winawer) but can be challenged by ...Bb4; **Nd2** (Tarrasch) keeps the c-pawn free to support d4 and avoids the ...Bb4 pin, but blocks the c1-bishop temporarily and is less active.
- **Queen:** g4 is a thematic aggressive square (especially in Winawer — Qg4 hitting g7 is the whole point of the Poisoned Pawn lines). Also e2, d2, sometimes h5.
- **Rooks:** kingside rook supports f4–f5; can swing via f1–f3–h3/g3 in attacking lines. Queenside rook supports queenside expansion (a3, b4, c-file battles).
- **King:** usually short, but in some Winawer lines White's king is also a question mark because the queenside pawns get shattered.

## Part 2 — The Advance Variation (3.e5)

**1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5**

White grabs space immediately and locks the centre. This is the most *strategically transparent* French: the battle lines are drawn instantly. It's hugely popular at club level because the plans are clear for White. **You must know this cold** because you'll face it constantly.

```
After 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5:

White chain: d4-e5 (points kingside →)
Black chain: d5-e6 (points queenside ←)

Black's target: d4 (the base). Break with ...c5.
White's target: e6/kingside. Build up, maybe f4-f5.
```

### 2.1 The main line and the critical tension on d4

**3...c5 4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3 Qb6**

This is the tabiya (standard starting position) of the Advance. Let's read every piece:

- **3...c5** — Black immediately attacks the base d4. *This is non-negotiable French strategy.*
- **4.c3** — White defends d4 with a pawn so that the d4-pawn is supported and the centre stays locked. (The alternative, 4.Nf3 or 4.dxc5, leads elsewhere; 4.c3 is the main line.)
- **4...Nc6** — second attacker on d4.
- **5.Nf3** — White defends d4 a second time and develops.
- **5...Qb6** — *third* attacker on d4, and it also hits b2. Now d4 is attacked by c5, Nc6, Qb6 (three) and defended by c3, Nf3, and the d-pawn is also... let's count defenders: the c3-pawn and the f3-knight defend d4 (the queen on d1 also notionally, but it's behind). The tension is real and this pawn is the whole game.

**White's main tries here:**

#### (A) 6.a3 — the modern main line (the "Milner-Barry-avoiding" flexible move)

**6.a3** prepares **b4** to gain queenside space and challenge Black's c5-pawn, and crucially takes the **b4-square** away from Black's pieces. After **6...c4** (Black locks the queenside, fixing the structure and planning ...Na5–b3 ideas and a ...f6 break) or **6...Nh6** (heading to f5 to pressure d4), play is rich.

- After **6...c4**, the centre is locked. Black's plan: **...Na5, ...Bd7, ...O-O-O sometimes, and the ...f6 break.** The knight heading to b3 (via a5) can be a thorn. White's plan: play on the kingside and try **b3** at some point to break Black's queenside chain, or reroute pieces to attack e6 and the kingside. White often plays **Nbd2, Be2, O-O, and prepares b3 or g4**.
- After **6...Nh6 7.b4 cxd4 8.cxd4 Nf5**, the knight on f5 hammers d4. White must be accurate. Note **7...cxd4 8.cxd4 Nf5** — the h6-knight wasn't a misplacement; **h6 was a transit square to the superb f5 outpost.** This answers "why is my knight on h6 and not f6?" — because f6 is blocked by nothing but the point is f5, and ...Nf6 would just hit the e5-pawn which is well defended, whereas f5 hits d4, the real target.

#### (B) 6.Be2 — solid development

White simply develops and intends O-O, keeping options. Black continues **6...Nh6** (to f5) or **6...cxd4 7.cxd4 Nh6** with the same f5 idea, or **6...Bd7** preparing ...Nge7-f5/g6 and ...Rc8.

#### (C) 6.Bd3?! — the move that invites the Milner-Barry Gambit

If **6.Bd3**, then **6...cxd4 7.cxd4 Bd7!** and now **8.O-O Nxd4!? 9.Nxd4 Qxd4** is the **Milner-Barry Gambit** — White sacrifices the d4-pawn for attacking chances against Black's king after **10.Nc3** (intending Nb5, Qe2, and pressure down the open lines, plus the Bxh7+ Greek gift ideas). **This is a critical tactical battleground — see Part 8.4.** Black can accept and defend accurately, or decline with **8...Nxd4** lines being the test.

### 2.2 White's plans in the Advance (the "what do I do" guide)

1. **Maintain the centre** (keep d4 and e5 alive). The e5-pawn cramps Black and is the basis of any kingside attack.
2. **Queenside space with a3 + b4** to gain space, fix Black's c5, and discourage Black's pieces from using b4/c4 actively — though if Black plays ...c4, White then aims for the **b3 break** to open the queenside.
3. **Kingside attack:** pieces toward h7/f7. The **Bd3 + Qd1-?/Ng5** battery, ideas of **Bxh7+** (Greek gift) if Black castles and the conditions are right, and sometimes a slow **f4–f5** plan to crack e6.
4. **Pressure the d5/e6 complex:** if Black ever plays ...cxd4 and White recaptures cxd4, the d4-pawn becomes an **isolated... no** — it's supported. But White can target e6 and d5 with pieces.
5. **The f5 break (by White):** in some structures White plays Nf3-h4 or pieces to enable f4–f5, cracking open e6 and the kingside.

> **White's typical move-10 plan if nothing forcing is happening:** finish development (Be2/Bd3, O-O, Nbd2, sometimes Re1 or Rb1), decide between the **b4/b3 queenside plan** and the **kingside attack**, and keep the e5-pawn defended. A very common, clean plan: **a3, b4, Be2, O-O, then Nbd2-b3 or Rb1 + b4-b5** to expand and pressure c6/the queenside, while watching for a kingside chance.

### 2.3 Black's plans in the Advance (the "what do I do" guide)

1. **Pressure d4** with ...c5, ...Nc6, ...Qb6, sometimes ...Nh6-f5 and ...cxd4 at the right moment. **The d4-pawn is your obsession.**
2. **Solve the bad bishop:** the standard route is **...Bd7** then **...Bb5** (trading light bishops — if White has a light bishop on d3 or e2, ...Bb5 offers a trade that *relieves Black's worst piece*) or ...Na5 and ...Bd7-b5. Trading light-squared bishops is a strategic triumph for Black.
3. **The ...f6 break:** strike the e5-head, especially once you've built up. After ...f6 exf6, recapture and you've freed your position and opened the f-file. Time it when your king is safe and your pieces are ready.
4. **Knight to f5** (via h6 or e7) to attack d4 and sit on a great square.
5. **Queenside play / ...c4:** sometimes Black locks with ...c4 to fix White's d4 as a permanent target and play ...Na5-b3, ...f6, and pile up.

> **Black's typical move-10 plan if nothing forcing is happening:** complete the standard setup — **...Bd7, ...Nge7 (or ...Nh6) heading to f5, ...Rc8, and prepare either ...f6 or ...cxd4 followed by piece pressure on d4.** A clean plan: **...Qb6, ...Bd7, ...Nge7, ...Nf5, ...Be7, ...O-O, then ...f6** — relentless pressure on d4 and e5.

### 2.4 The five questions, answered for the Advance tabiya (after 5...Qb6)

1. **What is each piece doing?** Black: c5/Nc6/Qb6 all attack d4 (the base); the c8-bishop is bad and wants to reach b5 via d7; the g8-knight will go to h6/e7 then f5 (to hit d4), not f6 (which only hits the well-defended e5). White: c3 + Nf3 hold d4, the f1-bishop wants d3 (attack h7) or e2, and White eyes a3–b4 and a kingside attack.
2. **What is the pawn structure telling you?** Locked chains: White has kingside space (e5), Black has the queenside/centre target d4. Black's break is ...c5/...f6 vs d4/e5; White's expansion is b4 and possibly f4–f5.
3. **Enemy weakness / mine?** White's weakness: the d4-base and the dark squares behind e5 if the centre dissolves. Black's weakness: the cramped kingside, the e6-pawn (target of f5), and the bad c8-bishop.
4. **What is the opponent threatening?** Right now after 5...Qb6, Black "threatens" pressure but nothing immediate; if White plays carelessly (e.g. develops the f1-bishop to a square that drops d4 or allows ...cxd4 winning material), ...cxd4 can win a pawn. White must keep d4 defended at every moment.
5. **Typical plan with nothing forcing?** See 2.2 (White) and 2.3 (Black) above.

## Part 3 — The Exchange Variation (3.exd5)

**1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.exd5 exd5**

White releases the central tension and creates a **symmetrical pawn structure** (pawns on d4/d5, everything else mirrored). This is often played by White to "kill" the French and draw, but it is *not* harmless and it is *not* automatically drawish — it just changes the question.

### 3.1 What the Exchange really is

The symmetry means **the bad-bishop problem disappears** — Black's c8-bishop is now free (it can go to f5/g4/e6 along open diagonals). This is the **good news for Black**: the Exchange is the one line where the French bishop is liberated for free.

But symmetry also means **whoever uses the open e-file and develops more purposefully gets the edge.** With best play it's roughly equal, but it's a real fight. White sometimes plays for a tiny edge with the bishop pair or queenside expansion; Black plays for piece activity and full equality (or more, if White is passive).

### 3.2 Main development

**4.Nf3 Nf6** (or 4.Bd3 / 4.c4 — see below)

- **4.Bd3** is the most testing try, developing the bishop to its best diagonal aiming at h7. Black mirrors: **4...Bd6** (or 4...Nc6 5.c3 Qh4!? hitting d4/h2 — a known equalizing trick). After **4...Bd6 5.Nf3 Nf6 6.O-O O-O 7.Bg5** (pinning) **7...Bg4!** Black gets active and equal.
- **4.c4** is the **"real" attempt at an advantage** — White turns it into an IQP (isolated queen's pawn) structure or a Panov-like fight. After **4...Nf6 5.Nc3 Be7 6.Nf3 O-O 7.c5!?** or **7.cxd5** lines, White plays for a small structural plus. Black equalizes with accurate development and timely ...c5/...Nc6 breaks.

### 3.3 Plans for both sides

**White's plans:**
1. **Develop actively, contest the e-file** (Re1, doubling rooks). The half-open/open e-file is the main feature.
2. **Bishop pair / minority attack:** sometimes White plays c4 and queenside expansion (b4–b5, the minority attack against Black's c-pawn to create a weakness on c6/d5), like a Carlsbad structure.
3. **Avoid total symmetry traps:** don't just mirror Black or you'll hand the initiative away after a tempo-gaining move.
4. **Target d5** if it becomes isolated (after c4xd5 ...Nxd5 or ...ex... structures), pile pieces on it.

**Black's plans:**
1. **Liberate and develop the c8-bishop actively** (to g4 pinning Nf3, or f5, or e6). *Enjoy your freed bishop.*
2. **Contest the e-file** symmetrically — get a rook to e8.
3. **...c5 break** to fight for the centre and avoid being squeezed; or ...Nc6 + ...Bg4 piling on d4.
4. **The ...Qh4 / ...Bg4 + ...Nc6 pressure tricks** against an inaccurate White (e.g. the 4.Bd3 Nc6 5.c3 Qh4 motif hitting d4 and h2/g2).

> **Move-10 plan, both sides:** finish developing (knights to f3/f6 and c3/c6, bishops to d3/d6 and active squares, castle, rooks to e1/e8). Then **White probes the queenside (minority attack) or piles on a potential isolated d5; Black equalizes with ...c5 or active piece play and contests the e-file.** If truly nothing is happening, improve your worst piece and double rooks on the e-file.

### 3.4 The five questions, Exchange

1. **Piece jobs?** The big change: Black's **c8-bishop is now a good piece** — develop it actively (g4/f5/e6). Knights to f6/c6 (or f3/c3), bishops eye the long diagonals and h2/h7. Rooks fight for the e-file.
2. **Structure?** Symmetrical d4/d5. No locked chain, so no "attack the base" theme — instead it's about **piece activity, the e-file, and possible IQP/minority-attack structures** if c4/c5 breaks happen.
3. **Weaknesses?** Initially none (symmetry). They're *created* — a backward c-pawn (minority attack), an isolated d-pawn (after c4xd5), or weak squares from a careless pawn push.
4. **Threats?** Usually slow. Watch for tactics on the open e-file (pins against an uncastled king, or Re1 winning a pinned piece), and the ...Qh4/Bxh7+ type shots if development lags.
5. **Plan with nothing forcing?** Improve pieces, seize the e-file, and prepare a minority attack (White) or the ...c5 freeing break and active bishop play (Black).

## Part 4 — The Tarrasch Variation (3.Nd2)

**1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nd2**

Named after Siegbert Tarrasch. White develops the knight to d2 instead of c3 for two reasons:
1. It **avoids the ...Bb4 pin** (which is the whole point of the Winawer).
2. It **keeps the c-pawn free** to support d4 with c3 if needed.

The cost: the d2-knight is **passive** (it blocks the c1-bishop temporarily and doesn't pressure d5/e4 like a c3-knight would). The Tarrasch is the choice of players who want a small, safe, positional edge without the wild complications of the Winawer. It's extremely respectable and was a favourite of Anatoly Karpov and Garry Kasparov as White.

### 4.1 Black's two main systems vs the Tarrasch

Black has a fundamental fork in the road here:

#### System 1: 3...Nf6 — the "Closed/main-line" Tarrasch

**3...Nf6 4.e5 Nfd7** and now the knight retreats to d7 (a transit/support square) because e5 kicked it. White continues **5.Bd3 c5 6.c3 Nc6 7.Ne2** (a key regrouping — the d2-knight goes to e2 then f3/g3, or supports the centre, and the structure resembles a better-version Advance for White because White got the extra tempo of Nd2-e2).

This leads to a **closed, Advance-like structure** where Black again hammers d4 with ...c5/...Nc6/...Qb6 and plays for ...f6, while White has a space edge and a kingside attack. The play is similar in spirit to the Advance (Part 2) — **the d4-base is again Black's target.**

- A famous **pawn-sacrifice resource for White:** after the main moves, White sometimes plays to give back/grab a pawn for activity; and Black has the thematic **...f6** and **...cxd4** breaks.
- **Key plan for Black:** ...c5, ...Nc6, ...Qb6, ...Be7 or ...g6 (to fianchetto the bad bishop's defender and prepare ...f6 — note the **...g6 + ...Bh6** or **...g6** setups appear here), ...O-O, ...f6.

#### System 2: 3...c5 — the "Open" Tarrasch (often leading to an IQP)

**3...c5** immediately challenges the centre. The main line **4.exd5** (or 4.Ngf3) **4...exd5** (or the sharp **4...Qxd5!?** — see below) **5.Ngf3 Nc6 6.Bb5** leads to an **Isolated Queen's Pawn (IQP)** position after ...cxd4 etc., where:
- **Black gets an isolated d5-pawn** but active piece play (the IQP gives space and activity in return for the long-term weakness).
- This is a completely different type of game — open, piece-active, tactical. If you like the IQP positions, this is a great choice for Black.

**The 4...Qxd5 line:** **3...c5 4.exd5 Qxd5!?** keeps the pawn structure more fluid and avoids the IQP. After **5.Ngf3 cxd4 6.Bc4 Qd6 7.O-O Nf6 8.Nb3** White has lead in development and pressure for the pawn; Black must develop carefully. This is a sharp, well-analysed battleground.

### 4.2 Plans for White in the Tarrasch

1. **Closed lines (vs 3...Nf6):** treat it like a favourable Advance. **Bd3 (aim at h7), c3, Ne2 regrouping (d2-knight to e2-f4/g3 or f3), O-O, then kingside expansion / attack and watch the centre.** The thematic **f4–f5** or piece play toward the king. Keep d4 solid.
2. **IQP lines (vs 3...c5 ...exd5):** if Black ends up with the isolated d5-pawn, **blockade d5** (knight to d4 is ideal), trade minor pieces to reach an endgame where the isolated pawn is just weak, and pressure it. *Pieces off = the isolani is weak; pieces on = the isolani gives the attacker activity*, so White trades pieces and Black keeps them on.
3. **The Bb5 pin** (against ...Nc6) is a recurring idea to damage Black's structure or trade off a defender.

### 4.3 Plans for Black in the Tarrasch

1. **Closed (3...Nf6):** the familiar French plan — **...c5, ...Nc6, ...Qb6, pressure d4, prepare ...f6.** Reroute the bad bishop (...b6/...Ba6, or ...Bd7-... depending on the position). Note that after the knight sits on d7, it also supports the ...c5 and ...f6 breaks and can reroute via f8/b6.
2. **Open (3...c5):** play for **active piece development around the IQP** — ...Nc6, ...Bd6/...Be7, ...O-O, ...Bg4 or ...Bf5 (the freed bishop!), and use the half-open e-file and the activity the isolani grants you. Aim your pieces at White's king.
3. **The bad bishop is much happier** in the open Tarrasch lines because the structure opens up — another reason ...c5 is attractive if you dislike passive positions.

> **Move-10 plan, Tarrasch:**
> - *Closed:* White = Ne2 regroup, O-O, kingside play, keep d4 solid; Black = ...Qb6, ...g6 or ...Be7, ...O-O, ...f6, pound d4.
> - *Open/IQP:* White = blockade d5 with a knight, trade minors, target the isolani; Black = maximize piece activity, keep pieces on, attack on the kingside/centre before the endgame arrives.

### 4.4 The five questions, Tarrasch (closed, after 3...Nf6 4.e5 Nfd7)

1. **Piece jobs?** Nd2 is passive but frees the c-pawn and dodges ...Bb4; it will reroute (Ne2). Black's Nf6 went to d7 because e5 kicked it — on d7 it supports ...c5 and ...f6 and can reroute. The c8-bishop is again the problem child (open lines later may free it). White's Bd3 eyes h7.
2. **Structure?** After 4.e5, it's an Advance-type locked chain — **target d4 (Black), expand kingside (White).**
3. **Weaknesses?** White: d4-base, slightly passive d2-knight. Black: cramped kingside, bad bishop, e6.
4. **Threats?** Generally slow and positional early; the immediate concern is just keeping d4 and e5 defended (White) and not letting White get a free kingside attack (Black).
5. **Plan with nothing forcing?** As in 4.2/4.3.

<a name="part-5"></a>
## Part 5 — The Classical & Steinitz Variations (3.Nc3 Nf6)

**1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Nf6**

With **3.Nc3**, White develops naturally, defends e4, and pressures d5. Black replies **3...Nf6**, adding pressure to e4. (The other main reply, **3...Bb4**, is the Winawer — Part 6.) Now White's most principled try is to push:

### 5.1 The Steinitz Variation: 4.e5

**4.e5 Nfd7** (the knight retreats to d7, as in the Tarrasch closed lines — *same idea, the f6-knight is kicked and reroutes to a useful square supporting ...c5/...f6*). **5.f4** (the **Steinitz**, named for Wilhelm Steinitz — White builds a big pawn centre d4/e5/f4) **5...c5 6.Nf3 Nc6 7.Be3** and we reach a major tabiya.

```
Steinitz centre: White pawns on d4, e5, f4 (huge space).
Black hammers the base again: ...c5, ...Nc6, ...Qb6.
```

This is one of the most important French structures. Let's read it:

- **White's big centre (d4–e5–f4)** grants enormous space and supports a kingside attack (f4–f5 is the dream break; pieces flow toward the king). But the centre is also a **target** — especially **d4**, attacked by ...c5/...Nc6/...Qb6.
- **Black's plan:** ...c5, ...Nc6, ...Qb6 (hitting d4 and b2), ...cxd4 at the right time, ...Be7, ...O-O (carefully — White's kingside attack is real), and the eternal ...f6 break to strike e5/f4.
- **The critical fight is over d4 and the timing of ...cxd4 and ...f6.**

**Main line continues 7...cxd4 8.Nxd4** and now **8...Bc5** (pinning/pressuring d4 and the e3-bishop) or **8...Qb6** with sharp play. A key tactical point: after ...cxd4 Nxd4, the **d4-knight can be attacked and traded**, and the battle for the dark squares and the e5-pawn intensifies. Black often achieves ...f6 to undermine e5.

> **Note on ...Qb6 and the b2-pawn:** Black's queen on b6 frequently eyes b2. White must decide whether to defend b2 (e.g. with Qd2, Rb1, or by accepting that ...Qxb2 leads to complications where the Black queen can get trapped or White gets compensation). The **"...Qxb2 — is it poisoned?"** question is a recurring tactical theme in Steinitz and Winawer alike (see Part 8).

### 5.2 The Classical main lines: 4.Bg5

Instead of 4.e5, White can play **4.Bg5**, pinning the f6-knight and increasing pressure on d5/e4. Black has two big replies:

#### (A) 4...Be7 — the classical Classical

**4...Be7 5.e5 Nfd7 6.Bxe7 Qxe7** (the pin is broken by trading; note White trades off the dark-squared bishops, which slightly relieves Black) **7.f4 O-O 8.Nf3 c5** with a Steinitz-like structure but with the dark-squared bishops off the board. Plans are similar: Black hits d4 (...c5/...Nc6/...Qb6/...a6 + ...b5 queenside expansion sometimes), White attacks on the kingside. The bishop trade slightly favours Black's defensive task.

#### (B) 4...dxe4 — the Burn Variation

**4...dxe4 5.Nxe4** (named after Amos Burn) leads to more open, less cramped positions. Black gives up the centre tension to free the game and reduce the bad-bishop problem. After **5...Be7 6.Bxf6 Bxf6** (or **5...Nbd7**), Black develops smoothly and the c8-bishop has better prospects (...b6/...Bb7 or ...Bd7-c6). This is a solid, less theory-heavy choice for Black that sidesteps the most cramped structures.

#### (C) 4...Bb4 — the McCutcheon Variation

**4...Bb4!?** (the **McCutcheon**) is a sharp, independent try: Black pins the c3-knight *and* attacks e4 without first defending the f6-knight. After **5.e5 h6 6.Bd2 Bxc3 7.bxc3 Ne4** the position becomes very concrete and double-edged — Black wrecks White's queenside pawns (...Bxc3) and plants a knight on e4, while White gets the bishop pair and central/kingside space. **This is a fighting weapon for Black who wants imbalance against 4.Bg5.** Lots of theory; very rewarding to know.

### 5.3 Plans for White in Classical/Steinitz

1. **Build and use the big centre:** d4/e5 (and f4 in Steinitz). The space supports a **kingside attack** — pieces toward h7/g7, the **f4–f5 break** to open lines, rook lifts (Rf1–f3–h3/g3), and Greek-gift Bxh7+ ideas if Black castles.
2. **Defend d4** adequately while attacking — the recurring tension. Often **Be3 + Qd2** holds d4 and connects for queenside castling or central defence.
3. **Trade off Black's good pieces / keep the c8-bishop bad.** The 4.Bg5 lines often trade dark-squared bishops, but White still tries to keep Black passive.
4. **Watch the ...f6 break** — when Black plays ...f6, be ready to either maintain e5 (with f4 and pieces) or open lines favourably with exf6.

### 5.4 Plans for Black in Classical/Steinitz

1. **Attack d4** — ...c5, ...Nc6, ...Qb6, ...cxd4. Relentlessly. This is the base of the chain.
2. **The ...f6 break** to undermine e5/f4 and open the f-file. Time it for when your king is safe and pieces support it.
3. **Solve the bad bishop:** in the Burn it's easier (open position, ...b6/...Bb7); in Steinitz it's the standard ...Bd7-... or ...b6 maneuvers; sometimes ...Bd7-e8-g6/h5.
4. **Queenside expansion** in some Classical lines: ...a6 and ...b5–b4 to attack the c3-knight and gain space, especially when White is committed to a kingside attack.
5. **Don't get mated:** be aware of White's kingside attack and the Greek gift. Sometimes Black delays castling or castles into a prepared defence (...h6, ...g6 setups, pieces covering the king).

> **Move-10 plan, Classical/Steinitz:**
> - *White:* keep the centre solid (Be3/Qd2 holding d4), complete development, then launch the kingside (f5 break, rook lift, piece attack), and look for Bxh7+ if the conditions arise.
> - *Black:* finish the ...c5/...Nc6/...Qb6 buildup, resolve the centre with ...cxd4 and/or ...f6 at the right moment, reroute the bad bishop, and generate queenside/central counterplay before White's attack lands.

### 5.5 The five questions, Steinitz (after 5...c5 6.Nf3 Nc6 7.Be3)

1. **Piece jobs?** Black: c5/Nc6/Qb6 attack d4; Nd7 supports c5/f6/e5 and reroutes (f8/b6); c8-bishop is bad (reroute later); Be7 develops and unpins concerns. White: the d4/e5/f4 centre grants space; Be3 defends d4; Nf3 defends d4/e5; the f1-bishop and queen aim at the kingside; Qd2 often connects rooks and adds a d4 defender.
2. **Structure?** Big White centre d4-e5-f4 = space + kingside attack; Black's target is **d4 (base)** and the head e5/f4 via ...f6.
3. **Weaknesses?** White: d4-base, the dark squares (especially if e5/f4 advance and leave holes), and an over-extended centre if Black breaks it. Black: cramped kingside, e6, bad bishop, king safety under the coming attack.
4. **Threats?** White is building toward f5 and a kingside attack; immediate threats are usually about the centre (don't let d4 fall) and tactical shots once pieces mass on the kingside. Black's ...Qb6 may threaten ...Qxb2 or ...cxd4 winning material if White is careless.
5. **Plan with nothing forcing?** As in 5.3/5.4.

## Part 6 — The Winawer Variation (3.Nc3 Bb4)

**1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4**

The **Winawer** (named after Szymon Winawer, deeply developed by Botvinnik, Tal, and modern players) is the **sharpest, most theoretically rich, and most double-edged** French line. Black pins the c3-knight, attacking e4 and threatening to capture on c3 to **wreck White's queenside pawn structure**. This is the variation that gives the French its dangerous, counterattacking reputation.

### 6.1 The main line and its soul

**4.e5** (White advances, gaining space and asking the bishop what it's doing) **4...c5** (immediately hitting the d4-base, as always) **5.a3** (challenging the bishop) **5...Bxc3+ 6.bxc3** and we reach the **defining Winawer structure**:

```
After 6.bxc3:
- White has DOUBLED, isolated-ish c-pawns (c2, c3) but the BISHOP PAIR
  and a big pawn centre (d4, e5) pointing at Black's kingside.
- Black has GIVEN UP the dark-squared bishop (and thus has potential
  DARK-SQUARE WEAKNESSES, especially around the kingside / c5 / e7),
  but has a SOUND pawn majority on the queenside and a clear target:
  White's d4-c3 pawns and the c-file.
```

**The fundamental imbalance:** Black gives up the dark-squared bishop and accepts dark-square weaknesses, in exchange for **damaging White's pawn structure** (doubled c-pawns) and getting a **clear queenside/central pawn target (d4, c3)** and the open c-file. White gets the **bishop pair**, a **big centre**, and a **kingside attack** (the dark-squared weaknesses around Black's king, especially g7, are inviting — hence **Qg4** is the key attacking move).

This single trade-off defines everything. Now:

**6...Ne7** (the knight goes to e7, not f6 — because f6 is unavailable/undesirable here; from e7 the knight defends g6/f5 and supports the kingside and can go to f5 to hit d4, and importantly it doesn't block the ...c5/...Qa5/...Qc7 queenside play) **7.Qg4** — the critical move.

### 6.2 The great fork: defend g7 or grab on c3?

After **7.Qg4**, White attacks **g7** (the dark-square weakness!). Black has two famous, completely different approaches:

#### (A) 7...Qc7 — the "Poisoned Pawn" Winawer / main line where Black lets g7 go

**7...Qc7!?** Black *ignores* the threat to g7 and instead supports ...cxd4 and the centre and prepares queenside play. After **8.Qxg7 Rg8 9.Qxh7 cxd4** Black has given up two kingside pawns (g7 and h7) but has a **huge initiative against White's centre and king** — the open g-file (rook on g8 hitting g2), the ...cxd4 break shattering White's centre, and rapid development with ...Nbc6, ...Bd7, ...O-O-O. **This is one of the most analysed and thrilling lines in all of chess.** It's a true gambit of kingside pawns for central/king-hunt counterplay.

This is **"the Poisoned Pawn" of the Winawer** (distinct from the Najdorf's). White grabs g7 and h7; Black hunts. Concrete knowledge is essential here — it's a memorization-heavy, razor-sharp line. **See Part 8 for the key tactical points.**

#### (B) 7...O-O — castling into it (the "Positional Winawer")

**7...O-O** Black castles, accepting that White can play **8.Bd3** (aiming at h7) and a kingside attack with the queen already on g4. After **8...Nbc6** (or 8...f5!? blunting the queen) Black defends and counterattacks on the queenside/centre. This is somewhat less wild than the Poisoned Pawn but still sharp.

#### (C) 7...Kf8 — the modern solid choice

**7...Kf8!?** keeps the structure intact (doesn't weaken with ...g6 or sacrifice with ...Qc7), giving up castling rights but keeping a very solid kingside. Black plays ...Nbc6, ...Bd7, ...Rc8/...Qa5 and pressures the queenside. A practical, sturdy modern approach favoured by players who don't want to memorize 25 moves of Poisoned Pawn theory.

#### (D) 7...cxd4 — capturing first

**7...cxd4 8.Qxg7 Rg8 9.Qxh7 Qc7** transposes to Poisoned-Pawn-type play. Move orders matter enormously in the Winawer; small differences change the evaluation.

### 6.3 Other Winawer move-5 and move-4 options

- **The Armenian / 5...Ba5** instead of 5...Bxc3+: Black keeps the bishop (maintaining the pin) at the cost of time. After **6.b4 cxd4 7.Qg4** or **6.b4 cxb4** sharp play. A way to avoid the main structure.
- **4.e5 b6!?** (the **"Black to play ...Ba6" idea**): Black prepares to trade the bad light-squared bishop immediately via ...Ba6, solving the French bishop problem at the cost of time. A respected sideline.
- **4.exd5** (the **Exchange Winawer**): **4...exd5** reaches an Exchange-type position but with bishops developed (Black's on b4); roughly equal, less critical.
- **4.a3** (the **"delayed" or anti-Winawer**): **4...Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 dxe4** and Black grabs the pawn; White plays for the bishop pair and development. The **Winckelmann–Riemer Gambit** territory.
- **4.Qg4!?** (the **"Winawer Gambit" / direct attack**): immediately hitting g7. **4...Nf6 5.Qxg7 Rg8 6.Qh6 Rg6 7.Qe3 cxd4** with wild play; or **4...Qd7** / **4...Kf8** solid. A tricky try.
- **4.Bd3 / 4.Nge2 / 4.Bd2** are quieter options; Black equalizes with ...dxe4 and development or ...c5.
- **4.e5 Qd7** and **4.e5 Ne7** move orders also exist.

### 6.4 Plans for White in the Winawer

1. **Use the bishop pair and the centre.** The **light-squared bishop to d3** (aiming at h7) plus the queen on g4/h5 is the attacking battery; the **dark-square weaknesses around Black's king (g7, h6, f6) are the targets.**
2. **Kingside attack:** Qg4 (hit g7), Bd3, h4–h5, Ng-stuff, rook lifts. Black's dark-square holes are the invitation. **This is White's main winning plan.**
3. **Hold the centre / accept the doubled pawns as dynamic:** the doubled c-pawns (c2, c3) support d4 (c3 defends d4) and the c2-pawn can later advance (c4) to challenge Black's d5 and open lines for the bishops.
4. **In the Poisoned Pawn:** grab g7/h7 and **defend accurately** against Black's initiative — return material if needed to reach a better endgame, or weather the storm and emerge a pawn up.
5. **The c3–d4 vs ...c5 tension** and the **c4 break** (White sometimes plays c2–c4 to undouble and activate the bishops) are key central resources.

### 6.5 Plans for Black in the Winawer

1. **Target White's d4 and c3 pawns** — ...c5, ...Nc6, ...Qa5 (hitting c3 and a-pawn, and eyeing the queenside), ...Qc7, ...cxd4 to open lines and damage/win the weak pawns. **White's queenside pawns are your obsession** (the mirror of "attack the base").
2. **Open the c-file** and pressure c3/c2 with rooks (...Rc8) and queen.
3. **The ...f6 / ...f5 breaks** vs e5: ...f6 to undermine the centre, or ...f5 (sometimes ...f5 then ...f4) to blunt White's attack and grab kingside space. In some lines ...f5 (gaining the e4-square and blunting the g4-queen) is excellent.
4. **Solve the bad bishop:** via ...b6 + ...Ba6 (trading it), or ...Bd7-... Sometimes the c8-bishop becomes important on the b1–h7 or a-file diagonals after the position opens.
5. **King safety decisions:** castle short (and defend), castle long (...O-O-O — common in Poisoned Pawn, putting the rook on the d-file/c-file for central play), or keep the king on f8 (the 7...Kf8 lines). **Choosing where the king lives is a defining Winawer decision** and depends on where White's attack is aimed.
6. **In the Poisoned Pawn:** rapid development (...Nbc6, ...Bd7, ...O-O-O), the rook on g8 hitting g2, ...d4-based central play, and a direct counterattack/king-hunt against White's exposed king and weak c-pawns. **Initiative is your compensation for the pawns — play fast and forcefully.**

> **Move-10 plan, Winawer:**
> - *White:* complete the attacking setup (Bd3, queen active on g4/h5, h4–h5, maybe Ne2/Nf3 and rook lifts), pressure the dark squares around Black's king, and convert the bishop pair; defend the c-pawns or push c4 to activate.
> - *Black:* pile on d4/c3 (...Nc6, ...Qa5/...Qc7, ...Rc8, ...cxd4), play the appropriate ...f6/...f5 break, get the king to safety in the right direction, and race your queenside/central counterplay against White's kingside attack.

### 6.6 The five questions, Winawer (after 6.bxc3 Ne7 7.Qg4)

1. **Piece jobs?** Black has **traded the dark-squared bishop** (for the c3-knight) to damage White's pawns — so Black's remaining minor pieces and queen must cover the **dark-square holes** (g7, e7-area) while attacking c3/d4. The e7-knight defends g6/f5 and supports the kingside (it went to e7, not f6, to avoid blocking queenside play and to guard the king). The c8-bishop is the usual problem (reroute via ...b6/...Ba6 or ...Bd7). The queen heads to a5/c7 to hit c3 and support ...cxd4. White's queen on g4 attacks g7 (the dark-square weakness); the f1-bishop wants d3 (h7 attack); the doubled c-pawns support d4 and may push c4.
2. **Structure?** The defining trade-off: White = doubled c-pawns + bishop pair + big centre + kingside attack; Black = sound queenside majority + open c-file + target on d4/c3 + dark-square weaknesses near the king. **Black attacks the queenside/centre; White attacks the kingside.**
3. **Weaknesses?** White: doubled, potentially weak c2/c3 pawns; the d4-pawn under fire. Black: dark squares around the king (g7, h6, f6, e7), and the bad light-squared bishop. Each side aims at the other's weakness.
4. **Threats?** After 7.Qg4, **White threatens Qxg7** winning a pawn and attacking the rook on h8/exposing Black's king. This is the immediate, concrete threat that forces Black's big decision (defend g7 with ...Qc7-letting-it-go-as-a-gambit, castle, ...Kf8, or ...g6/...Ng6 type defences). *This is exactly the kind of "what is my opponent threatening RIGHT NOW" moment that decides games.*
5. **Plan with nothing forcing?** As in 6.4/6.5 — but in the Winawer something forcing is *usually* happening; this is the least "quiet" French.

## Part 7 — Sidelines You Must Know

These aren't main lines, but you'll meet them, and several contain nasty traps.

### 7.1 The King's Indian Attack (KIA) — 2.d3 or 2.Qe2-ish setups / 1.e4 e6 2.d3

**1.e4 e6 2.d3** (or via 2.Nf3 d5 3.Nbd2 / 3.Qe2) is the **King's Indian Attack**, beloved by Bobby Fischer. White declines to contest the centre with d4 and instead plays a reversed King's Indian: **Nd2, Ngf3, g3, Bg2, O-O, Re1, e5 (advancing the e-pawn at the right moment), then a kingside attack with Qe2, h4, Nf1-g3-f5 / Bf4, etc.**

- **The key moment:** White plays **e4–e5** (often after Black has committed pawns), gaining a kingside space chain and attacking chances; this can transpose to French-like structures but with White's setup flipped.
- **Black's plan:** since White doesn't fight for the centre, Black should **seize it** — **...d5, ...c5, ...Nc6, ...Bd6 or ...Be7, ...b5–b4 queenside expansion**, and play on the queenside (where Black is faster) while defending the kingside. A common strong setup for Black: **...d5, ...Bd6, ...Nge7, ...O-O, ...b5, ...a5, ...b4** — a queenside pawn storm racing White's kingside one. The **...b5-b4** break hitting White's knight on d2/c3-area is thematic.
- **Critical defensive note for Black:** be ready for White's kingside attack (e5, Qe2, h4-h5, Nf5, Bf4-h6). Don't get mated while you're busy on the queenside. Trading off White's attacking pieces (especially the light-squared g2-bishop or the f5-knight) defuses the attack.

> **Five questions, KIA (Black's view):** White isn't contesting the centre → **take it** (the structure tells you the centre and queenside are yours); White's attack is on the kingside (his target is your king), your target is the queenside and White's slightly loose centre; White threatens a slow build toward e5 and a kingside pawn storm, so develop with that in mind; your no-forcing plan is the **...b5-b4 queenside expansion + solid centre**.

### 7.2 The Wing Gambit — 1.e4 e6 2.Nf3 d5 3.e5 c5 4.b4!?

After an Advance-like setup, **4.b4** (the **Wing Gambit**) sacrifices the b-pawn to deflect Black's c5-pawn away from the d4-base, hoping to build an unchallenged centre. **4...cxb4** (accepting) **5.a3 (or 5.d4)** and White plays for central/development compensation. **Black should usually accept** and then **return the pawn at the right moment to complete development and keep the extra structure/centre**, or hold the pawn while developing solidly (...d5 is already played, ...Nc6, ...Bd7/...a5 to hold b4, ...Ne7). With accurate play Black is fine or better — the gambit is dubious but tricky.

### 7.3 2.Qe2 — Chigorin's anti-French

**1.e4 e6 2.Qe2!?** (Chigorin's idea) prevents an immediate ...d5 (because 2...d5 3.exd5 would leave Black's e-pawn... actually after 2...d5 3.exd5 Qxd5? loses time and 3...exd5 leaves an isolated-ish structure; more to the point 2.Qe2 means 2...d5 can be met by 3.exd5 with a slight pull) and aims for a KIA-like setup with the queen oddly placed. Black does well with **2...c5** (transposing toward a kind of reversed/odd Sicilian) or **2...Be7** then ...d5 when favourable. Not dangerous with care, but unusual — don't autopilot ...d5 without checking the recapture.

### 7.4 The Two Knights / 2.Nf3 d5 3.Nc3

**1.e4 e6 2.Nf3 d5 3.Nc3** can transpose to many things; **3...Nf6** or **3...d4** (kicking the c3-knight and grabbing space — a respectable independent try) **4.Ne2 c5** gives Black a good game with central space. Watch for transpositions back into Classical/Advance structures.

### 7.5 The Rubinstein Variation — 3...dxe4 (vs both 3.Nc3 and 3.Nd2)

**1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 dxe4** (or 3.Nd2 dxe4) **4.Nxe4** is the **Rubinstein Variation** — Black voluntarily gives up the centre to obtain a **solid, slightly passive but very sound** position and, importantly, to **ease the bad-bishop problem** (the position opens enough for the c8-bishop to breathe via ...Bd7-c6 or ...b6-Bb7). After **4...Nd7 5.Nf3 Ngf6 6.Nxf6+ Nxf6 7.Bd3 c5** (or 4...Bd7 intending ...Bc6) Black reaches a sturdy structure. **This is the choice for French players who want safety and to avoid heavy theory** — at the cost of a bit of passivity and conceding the centre. It's fully respectable and hard to crack.

> **Five questions, Rubinstein:** the c8-bishop's job is finally solvable (reroute to c6 via d7, or b7 via b6) — that's the *point* of the line; the structure is open-ish with White holding a small space edge; Black's weakness is slight passivity, White's is essentially nothing major but no real attack either; threats are minimal early; the no-forcing plan is **smooth development, ...c5 to free the game, and equalize via piece activity and the freed bishop.**

## Part 8 — Traps, Gambits & Tactical Motifs Catalogue

This is your "don't lose to a trick, and spring a few of your own" section. **Question 4 ("what is my opponent threatening right now?") lives here.** Memorize the patterns.

### 8.1 The Greek Gift Sacrifice — Bxh7+ (White's recurring kingside shot)

The **single most important tactic for White in the French** (Advance, Tarrasch, Classical) and the **single most important thing for Black to watch for.**

**The pattern:** White has a bishop on d3 (aiming at h7), a knight ready to jump to g5, and a queen that can reach the h-file (often via Qd1-... or the d1-h5 route after the f3-knight moves, or Qg4-h5). Black has castled short with a pawn on h7 and *insufficient defenders*. Then:

**1. Bxh7+! Kxh7 2. Ng5+** and now:
- **2...Kg8 3. Qh5** threatens Qh7# (and Qxf7+); Black is often mated or loses heavy material.
- **2...Kg6** (trying to hold the piece) walks into attacks like **3. Qg4 / h4-h5+** with a deadly king hunt.
- **2...Kh6 3. Nxf7+ / Qg4** etc.

**When it works (White's checklist):** bishop bears on h7, a knight reaches g5 with tempo, the queen can reach h5/h-file fast, and Black lacks the key defenders (...Nf6 covering h7/h5, or ...a defender of g5, or a luft/escape). **When Black has ...Nf6 firmly defending h7 and h5, or can meet Ng5 with ...Bxg5 / ...Qxg5, the gift is usually unsound.**

> **Black's defense rule:** before castling short into a White Bd3 + open kingside, ask **"can White play Bxh7+ then Ng5+ and Qh5?"** Keep a defender on f6 (knight), avoid removing the f6-knight at the wrong time, and watch the g5-square. If you can't answer the gift, don't castle into it yet, or pre-empt with ...h6 (carefully) or by trading the d3-bishop (...Bb5 or ...Ba6 ideas, or ...Nb4 hitting it).

### 8.2 ...Qb6xb2 — Is the b2-pawn poisoned? (both colours)

In many French lines (Steinitz, Winawer, Advance), Black's queen reaches b6 and eyes **b2**. Grabbing it can:
- **Win a pawn** if White is careless, **OR**
- **Trap the queen** or grant White a huge initiative/development lead if it's a poisoned pawn.

**Black's checklist before ...Qxb2:** after ...Qxb2, can White trap the queen (e.g. Na4 hitting the queen and threatening to corner it on b2/a1, or Rb1 then Rb2 ideas, or Nb5-d6+ forks)? Is White's resulting initiative worth more than the pawn? **In sharp lines, ...Qxb2 is often playable but must be calculated to the end — it's frequently a "grab and run" where the queen must find a safe path back (...Qb2-a3 or ...-b6 etc.).** When in doubt and behind in development, **don't grab b2.**

**White's checklist:** if Black plays ...Qxb2, look for **Na4 (attacking the queen), Rb1 (with Rb2/Rxb7 ideas), and Nb5** tactics to trap the queen or win time. Sometimes sacrificing b2 deliberately (a real gambit) for a development lead and attack is White's best practical try.

### 8.3 The Winawer Poisoned Pawn — key tactical landmarks

**1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 c5 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Ne7 7.Qg4 Qc7 8.Qxg7 Rg8 9.Qxh7 cxd4**

This is a forcing, memorization-heavy line. The key ideas:
- White is **two pawns up** (g7, h7) but **behind in development and his king is shaky.**
- Black's compensation: **the open g-file (Rg8 hits g2), the shattered White centre (...cxd4 hitting c3), rapid development (...Nbc6, ...Bd7, ...O-O-O), and a direct attack.**
- A critical resource for White is **10.Ne2** (defending and developing, eyeing the d4/f4 squares and protecting against ...Qxc3+/...dxc3) and ideas of **Qd3/Qh5** to return to defense and consolidate the extra pawns.
- A critical idea for Black is **...Qxc3+** (or **...dxc3**) hitting White's structure, **...Nbc6** hitting d4/e5, **...Bd7 + ...O-O-O** to mass on the c/d-files, and **...d4-d3** or **...Nf5** ideas to keep the initiative.
- **Both sides must know concrete moves here** — this is the one French line where "understanding" alone isn't enough; you need memorized theory because the lines are forcing and a single inaccuracy can be fatal for either side.

> **Practical advice:** if you don't want to memorize 20+ moves of theory, play **7...Kf8** or **7...O-O** instead of the Poisoned Pawn as Black, and as White consider **avoiding the main Poisoned Pawn** with a different move 7 (e.g. 7.h4 or 7.Nf3) if you prefer less forcing play.

### 8.4 The Milner-Barry Gambit — White's Advance-Variation gambit

**1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3 Qb6 6.Bd3 cxd4 7.cxd4 Bd7 8.O-O Nxd4 9.Nxd4 Qxd4**

White sacrifices the d4-pawn. **White's compensation:** a big development lead and attacking chances against Black's king. After **10.Nc3** (threatening **Nb5** hitting the queen and c7/d6, and preparing **Qe2/Re1/Bxh7+** kingside ideas), White's pieces flood out while Black is undeveloped and the king is in the centre.

- **The main trap:** if Black is greedy or careless (e.g. **10...Qxe5??** grabbing a second pawn) White gets **11.Re1** winning the queen or crashing through. Even **10...a6** (stopping Nb5) is met by **11.Qe2** with strong pressure (threatening Bxh7+ and quick development).
- **Black's correct path:** return the pawn at the right moment and **complete development safely** — e.g. lines with **10...a6 11.Qe2 Ne7** (developing, heading to castle/cover the kingside) or the modern **10...Qxe5** is dangerous; safer is to give material back to develop. Accurate defense gives Black a good game (the gambit is objectively dubious), but **practically it's very dangerous and scores well for White at club level.**

> **Black's rule vs Milner-Barry:** *Don't be greedy.* You're up a pawn — your job is to **develop and king-safety**, returning the pawn if it buys you time. Watch for **Nb5, Qe2, Re1, and Bxh7+.**

### 8.5 The Winawer / Qg4 traps against ...g7

After **...Bxc3+ bxc3** and an early **Qg4**, if Black ever defends g7 with **...g6?!** it can create dark-square weaknesses (h6, f6) that White exploits with **h4-h5, Bg5/Bh6, and piece play.** Conversely, if Black tries to hold everything materially without counterplay, White's attack rolls. **The lesson: in the Winawer, counterplay (against c3/d4) is mandatory — passive defense loses.**

### 8.6 The Burn / Classical ...Ne4 and ...Bxc3 motifs (McCutcheon)

In the McCutcheon (**4.Bg5 Bb4 5.e5 h6 6.Bd2 Bxc3 7.bxc3 Ne4**), Black's knight on e4 and the doubled White c-pawns mirror the Winawer trade-off. Tactical points revolve around **the e4-knight (can White kick it with f3/Bd3, and is it well-supported?)**, **White's Qg4 hitting g7** (again the dark squares!), and **Black's ...c5 and queenside play vs White's bishop pair/center.** Know the main line **8.Qg4 g6 9.Bd3 Nxd2 10.Kxd2!?** (or 10.Qxd2) with sharp, balanced play.

### 8.7 General French tactical alarms (memorize these triggers)

- **Bxh7+ Greek gift** whenever White has Bd3 + Ng5-ready + queen-to-h5-ready and you've castled short without an f6-defender. **(Black: watch always.)**
- **Nb5 forks/threats** hitting c7, d6, a7 — common when the c-file opens and Black's queen/king are loose (Milner-Barry, Advance, Winawer).
- **...Qxb2 / ...Qxc3+** grabs — calculate queen safety to the end.
- **f5 breaks** (by White) cracking e6 — when White achieves f4-f5, the e6-pawn and the light squares around Black's king can collapse; Black must be ready to meet f5 with ...exf5 or piece blockades.
- **...f6 breaks** (by Black) — after ...f6 exf6, watch for tactics on the newly opened e-file and f-file, pins on e6/e7, and the e5/e6 square fights.
- **d-file pins and the e-file** in the Exchange and IQP lines — pieces pinned against the king or queen on open central files.
- **Back-rank and Rg8/g2 motifs** in the Poisoned Pawn — the open g-file is a weapon for Black.
- **Trapped bad bishop** — occasionally Black's c8-bishop or a misplaced piece gets trapped on the queenside after ...b6/...Ba6 if not careful (e.g. a bishop on a6 cut off, or hit by Nb5xa6-area ideas / b-pawn pushes). Watch your own piece coordination.

## Part 9 — The Five Questions: A Repeatable Framework

Use this **every move** (or at least at every critical moment). It's the antidote to "I have no idea what to do." Run it in order.

### Question 1 — "What is each piece trying to do?"

Go piece by piece. For the **French specifically**, the high-value checks are:
- **My light-squared bishop (if I'm Black):** what's its plan — trade (via b5/a6/b6-Ba6), reroute (d7-c6/e8-g6), or wait? *If it has no plan, find one — this is usually the source of a bad French position.*
- **My dark-squared bishop:** is it my good piece? Don't trade it without a concrete gain (damaging pawns, removing a key defender/attacker).
- **My knights:** is each on its best square? (Black: knight to f5 hits d4; knight to c6 hits d4; knight to d7 supports breaks. White: Nf3 holds the centre; a knight to b5 can fork; Nd2/Ne2 reroutes.) **"Why this square and not that one?"** — almost always the answer is *"because this square supports the thematic break or attacks the key target (d4 for Black, the kingside/e6 for White)."**
- **My queen:** Black's queen loves b6/a5/c7 (pressure d4/c3/b2); White's queen loves g4/h5/d2/e2 (attack or central support).
- **My rooks:** are they on (or aiming for) the file my break will open? (...c-file via ...cxd4; ...f-file via ...f6; the e-file in Exchange/IQP.)

### Question 2 — "What is the pawn structure telling me?"

- **Identify the chains.** Locked centre (e5 present)? Then: **base of White's chain = d4** (Black's target), **base of Black's chain = e6** (White's target). Symmetrical (Exchange)? Then it's about **piece activity and the e-file.** IQP (Tarrasch open)? Then it's **blockade-and-target (the side without the IQP) vs activity (the side with it).**
- **Where do I have space?** White usually kingside (e5 chain); Black usually queenside/centre (the d4 target + queenside majority in the Winawer).
- **Which break helps ME?** Black: **...c5** (vs d4) almost always; **...f6** (vs e5) when king-safe and supported. White: **f4–f5** (vs e6) and queenside expansion (b4) in the Advance; the **c4** break in the Winawer/Exchange to activate.
- **Which break helps my OPPONENT, and how do I prepare for it?** (Black must be ready for White's f5; White must be ready for Black's ...c5/...f6.)

### Question 3 — "Where is the enemy weakness, and where is mine?"

- **Enemy weaknesses to aim at:**
  - vs locked-centre White: **the d4-pawn (base)**, the **dark squares behind e5** if the centre dissolves, and an **over-extended centre.**
  - vs Winawer White: the **doubled c-pawns (c2/c3)** and **d4.**
  - vs any White: a **stuck/undeveloped piece**, a **weak back rank**, the **king if it's stuck in the centre or has a loose pawn cover.**
- **My weaknesses to defend/cover:**
  - as Black: the **e6-pawn** (f5 target), the **bad light-squared bishop**, the **cramped kingside / king safety** (Greek gift!), and in the Winawer the **dark squares around the king.**
  - as White: the **d4-base**, the **doubled c-pawns** (Winawer), **over-extension** if you push too many pawns.

### Question 4 — "What is my opponent threatening RIGHT NOW?" (the trap-preventer)

**Before every move, scan for forcing replies your opponent has:**
- **Checks** (does the opponent have a check that wins material or mates? — e.g. Nb5+ forks, Bxh7+ Greek gift, Qxg7/back-rank ideas).
- **Captures** (can the opponent win a pawn or piece — e.g. ...cxd4 winning the base if you under-defended it; ...Qxb2/...Qxc3+ grabs; a capture that opens a deadly file).
- **Threats of the above next move** (is the opponent setting up Bxh7+? Building f4-f5? Maneuvering a knight to b5 or f5? Lining up on an open file?).

**The French-specific "RIGHT NOW" alarms** (cross-reference Part 8):
1. **Greek gift Bxh7+** (Black: is my king safe? do I have the f6-defender?).
2. **...cxd4 winning the d4-pawn** (White: is d4 defended enough this very move?).
3. **Nb5 forks** when the c-file/queenside is loose.
4. **Qxg7 in the Winawer** (the pawn grab that forces a decision).
5. **f5 break** cracking e6 (Black: am I ready to meet f5?).
6. **...Qxb2 / ...Qxc3+** queen grabs (both: is the queen safe afterward?).

> **If you do nothing else, do this:** every move, ask **"if I pass, what would my opponent play?"** That single habit prevents the vast majority of trap-losses in the French.

### Question 5 — "What is my typical plan if nothing forcing is happening?"

When there's no immediate tactic, **improve toward your thematic plan** (see each Part's "move-10 plan"):
- **Black, locked centre (Advance/Steinitz/Tarrasch-closed):** build pressure on **d4** (...c5, ...Nc6, ...Qb6, ...Nf5, ...cxd4), **solve the bad bishop** (...Bd7-b5 or ...b6-Ba6), and prepare the **...f6 break.** Improve your worst piece; double on the c-file (or f-file after ...f6).
- **White, locked centre:** keep **d4 solid**, finish development, choose between the **queenside plan (a3-b4, or b3 break)** and the **kingside attack (f5 break, piece storm, Greek-gift watch)**, and improve your worst piece.
- **Exchange/Rubinstein:** improve pieces, **seize the open file** (e-file), prepare the **freeing/activating break** (...c5 for Black, c4/minority attack for White), and activate the (now good) light-squared bishop.
- **Winawer:** Black races queenside/central counterplay vs c3/d4 while choosing king safety; White presses the kingside (dark squares) with the bishop pair. Improve toward your attack; in this line "nothing forcing" is rare.

> **The meta-rule:** *In a locked French, the side that correctly identifies "my side of the board" and improves toward the right break, while answering Question 4 every move, will not be lost — and will usually be better.*

<a name="part-10"></a>
## Part 10 — The "After Move 10" Survival Guide

You asked specifically not to feel clueless after move 10. Here is the distilled, no-nonsense guide. Find your structure, follow the plan, run the five questions.

### If the centre is LOCKED (e5 on the board) — Advance, Steinitz, Tarrasch-closed, Classical-with-e5

**You are Black:**
1. Is **d4** under maximum pressure? If not, add an attacker (...Nc6, ...Qb6, ...Nf5 via h6/e7, ...cxd4 at the right time). **d4 is your sun; orbit it.**
2. Is your **bad bishop** solved or solving? If not, start the plan (...Bd7 heading to b5, or ...b6 heading to a6). **Don't leave it dead on c8.**
3. Is your king safe enough to play **...f6**? If yes and your pieces support it, break with ...f6 to undermine e5. If not, prepare it (pieces first).
4. **Every move:** Question 4 — watch for **Bxh7+**, **f5**, and **Nb5**.
5. Rooks to **c8** (and **f8** if you'll play ...f6).

**You are White:**
1. Is **d4** safe *this move*? Keep it defended (c3, Nf3, Be3, sometimes Qd2). **If d4 falls, your whole space advantage collapses.**
2. Pick your side: **kingside attack** (Bd3 at h7, f4-f5 break, rook lift, Greek-gift watch) or **queenside** (a3-b4, b5, or the b3 break vs ...c4). Commit and improve toward it.
3. **Keep Black's bad bishop bad** — avoid trades that free it; trade Black's *good* pieces.
4. **Every move:** Question 4 — watch for **...cxd4** (winning d4 if you slip), **...f6** (be ready to meet it), and **...Qxb2.**
5. Rooks support your plan (f-file/kingside, or b-file/c-file).

### If the centre is SYMMETRICAL — Exchange

1. **Develop actively and grab the e-file** (rook to e1/e8). 
2. Black: **enjoy the freed light-squared bishop** (g4/f5/e6). White: consider the **minority attack (b4-b5)** or play on a future isolated d-pawn.
3. Prepare the **...c5 break** (Black, to free fully) or **c4/queenside** (White).
4. **Every move:** Question 4 — watch for **e-file pins/tactics** and **...Qh4/Bxh7+** if development lags.

### If it's an IQP (Tarrasch open, ...c5 lines) 

1. **Side WITH the IQP (usually Black here):** keep pieces ON, play actively, attack (especially the kingside), use your space; the isolani gives you activity now in exchange for a long-term weakness — **make the activity count before the endgame.**
2. **Side WITHOUT the IQP (usually White):** **blockade the isolani** (knight to d4/d5), **trade pieces**, and **target the weak pawn** to win it in a simplified position.
3. **Every move:** Question 4 — watch for tactical shots the active side gets from the open lines (the IQP side often has Bxh7+/...Bxh2+ ideas, rook lifts, and piece sacrifices on the kingside).

### If it's the WINAWER structure (doubled White c-pawns, Black's dark-squared bishop gone)

1. **Black:** pile on **c3/d4** (...Nc6, ...Qa5/...Qc7, ...Rc8, ...cxd4), choose your **king's home** (f8, short, or long) based on where White attacks, play the right **...f6/...f5** break, and **race your counterplay.** **Passivity loses — counterattack is mandatory.**
2. **White:** attack the **dark squares around Black's king** with the **bishop pair and queen (Qg4/h5, Bd3, h4-h5)**, watch your **c-pawns**, and consider the **c4** break to activate. **Your bishop pair + attack is your trump.**
3. **Every move:** Question 4 — for White, **Qxg7** and dark-square shots; for Black, the safety of grabbed pawns (...Qxc3+) and White's attacking buildup. In the Poisoned Pawn, **know your theory** — it's forcing.

### The universal post-move-10 checklist (print this)

```
EVERY MOVE, IN ORDER:
1. Did my opponent just create a THREAT? (check/capture/setup) → deal with it.
2. Is my key target under max pressure? (Black: d4 / White: e6 or Black's king)
3. Is my worst piece improving? (Black: the c8-bishop, almost always)
4. Which BREAK am I working toward, and is now the moment? (...c5/...f6 // f5/b4/c4)
5. Is my king safe against the thematic shot? (Greek gift Bxh7+ above all)
If 1–5 are answered, make the move that best advances YOUR side's plan.
```

## Part 11 — Study Plan & Model Games

### 11.1 A practical study order

1. **Master Part 1** (the soul) until the four structural truths are automatic.
2. **Learn the Advance (Part 2) cold** — it's the most common at club level and teaches the core chain-and-break logic.
3. **Learn the Exchange (Part 3)** — simple structurally but you must avoid drawish passivity.
4. **Pick your weapon vs 3.Nc3:** either the **Classical 3...Nf6** (Part 5) or the **Winawer 3...Bb4** (Part 6). The Winawer is sharper and higher-reward but theory-heavy; the Classical is more positional. **Or** adopt the **Rubinstein 3...dxe4** (Part 7.5) as a low-theory solid system vs both 3.Nc3 and 3.Nd2.
5. **Learn the Tarrasch (Part 4)** — decide between the closed (3...Nf6) and open (3...c5) approaches.
6. **Drill the traps (Part 8)** until the alarm patterns (Greek gift, ...Qxb2, Nb5, f5, Poisoned Pawn landmarks, Milner-Barry) are reflexive.
7. **Internalize the five-question framework (Part 9)** and the move-10 guide (Part 10) by applying them to your own games.

### 11.2 Choosing a Black repertoire (decision guide)

- **Want maximum solidity, minimum theory?** → **Rubinstein (3...dxe4)** vs 3.Nc3/3.Nd2, and standard ...c5 plans vs Advance/Exchange.
- **Want a positional fight with clear plans?** → **Classical (3...Nf6, then ...Be7 or the Burn 4...dxe4)**, ...c5 vs Advance, ...Bd6 vs Exchange.
- **Want sharp, winning-attempt counterattack?** → **Winawer (3...Bb4)**; decide between the **Poisoned Pawn (memorize)** and the **solid 7...Kf8/7...O-O** lines.
- **Vs the Tarrasch:** match it to taste — **3...Nf6 (closed, French-typical)** or **3...c5 (open, IQP, active).**

### 11.3 Model games to study (search these out)

These are classics that illustrate the themes. Look them up in a database and play through them with the five questions in mind:

- **Advance & chain-play:** Nimzowitsch's French games (he pioneered the blockade and chain theory). Look for **Nimzowitsch vs Salwe, Carlsbad 1911** (the model blockade game) and **Nimzowitsch's writings in *My System*** on the French pawn chain.
- **Winawer fireworks:** **Botvinnik's** Winawer games (he was the great theoretician of the Black side); **Tal's** attacking Winawers; modern **Winawer Poisoned Pawn** games in any current database.
- **Classical/Steinitz:** **Steinitz's** original big-centre games; modern Classical games for current handling.
- **Tarrasch (White's positional squeeze):** **Karpov's** Tarrasch games as White (model technique); **Kasparov** also used it.
- **Exchange (making it a fight):** look for games where Black generates winning chances with active piece play to dispel the "Exchange = draw" myth.

> **How to study a model game (the five-question method):** at each critical moment, pause and answer the five questions yourself *before* seeing the move played. Then compare. This trains the exact decision-making you asked for — so that after move 10 (or move 30), you always have a plan and never feel clueless.

### Final word

The French Defence rewards **understanding over memorization** in almost every line — *except* the Winawer Poisoned Pawn and the Milner-Barry, where concrete knowledge is essential. If you internalize:

1. **the chain logic** (attack the base: d4 for Black, e6/kingside for White),
2. **the bad-bishop question** (solve it or make it irrelevant),
3. **your two breaks** (...c5 and ...f6 for Black; f5/b4/c4 for White),
4. **the trap alarms** (Greek gift above all, plus ...Qxb2, Nb5, f5, the Poisoned Pawn and Milner-Barry landmarks), and
5. **the five-question habit** (especially Question 4 every single move),

…you will have a complete, plan-driven French — as both colours — with no "clueless after move 10" gaps. Study it, drill the patterns, and apply the framework to your own games.

*Good luck, and enjoy the richest strategic battleground in all of 1.e4 e5... er, 1.e4 e6.*
