## Part 1 — Why the Scandinavian: The Big Picture

The Scandinavian (a.k.a. Center Counter) is the most *direct* response to `1.e4`. Black immediately challenges the e4-pawn and, in the main lines, trades it off. This has three big consequences that define everything else:

**1. Black gets a clean, symmetrical-ish pawn structure with no permanent weaknesses.** After `1.e4 d5 2.exd5 Qxd5`, Black has pawns on c7-e7-f7-g7-h7-a7-b7 — a healthy structure. There is no backward pawn, no isolani (yet), nothing for White to chew on long-term. This is why the Scandinavian is *solid*. You are very rarely going to get slowly squeezed and lose without counterplay.

**2. Black accepts a small, temporary cost: the queen comes out early (in the ...Qxd5 lines) or a tempo is spent (in the ...Nf6 lines).** White gains time by harassing the queen with `Nc3`. The entire theoretical battle of the main line is: *can White convert that tempo into a lasting initiative before Black completes development?* The answer at the club and even master level is: usually no, if Black knows the plans.

**3. Black almost always gets the bishop pair question resolved early and develops the light-squared bishop actively to f5 or g4** — *outside* the pawn chain. This is the single most important strategic feature of the opening and it solves the classic "bad French/Caro bishop" problem before it ever arises.

### The Scandinavian "deal" in one sentence
> *Black says: "I'll let you gain a tempo on my queen, and in exchange I get a rock-solid structure, a good light-squared bishop, and an easy, plan-based middlegame where I always know what I'm doing."*

This is why it's such a good practical weapon, especially for players who hate memorizing 20 moves of theory and want to *understand* their position instead.

### Who should play it
- **As Black:** anyone who wants a reliable, low-theory, plan-rich answer to 1.e4 that avoids the Ruy Lopez, Italian, and the giant theory of the Sicilian.
- **As White:** you don't "choose" to face it, but this study teaches you exactly how to play *against* it for an edge (Part 6 White sections + Part 4.8).

## Part 2 — The Two Families

Everything in the Scandinavian splits into two families. Know which one you're in at all times.

### Family A — The Queen Recapture: `1.e4 d5 2.exd5 Qxd5`
Black grabs the pawn back immediately with the queen. White plays `3.Nc3` hitting the queen, and Black chooses where the queen goes: **a5, d6, d8** (or the dubious e5+). This is the **classical Scandinavian** and the bulk of real-world games. Solid, strategic, plan-based.

### Family B — The Knight Recapture / Delayed Recapture: `1.e4 d5 2.exd5 Nf6`
Black does **not** grab back immediately. Instead the knight goes to f6 to attack the d5-pawn, planning to recapture with the piece (or provoke White into trying to hold the extra pawn, leading to gambits). This is the **Modern Scandinavian**, and it branches into the sharp gambit lines (Portuguese, Icelandic) that are dangerous if White is unprepared.

> **Mental model:** Family A is "I want a good game with no weaknesses." Family B is "I want to fight for the initiative and maybe gambit a pawn for fast development and traps."

You can play *either* as your main weapon. This study covers both fully so you understand both sides of the board.

## Part 3 — Universal Concepts (Read Before The Lines)

These ideas recur in almost every Scandinavian. Internalize them and half the moves play themselves.

### 3.1 The light-squared bishop is the soul of the position
In the ...Qxd5 main lines Black almost always plays an early `...Bf5` or `...Bg4` **before** locking it in with `...e6`. This is the whole point: unlike the French or Caro-Kann where this bishop can become a sad prisoner, here it gets out first.

- **...Bf5** — eyes the b1-h7 diagonal, controls e4, supports a future `...e6/...c6` setup. The most common and solid choice.
- **...Bg4** — pins or pressures the f3-knight, trades to damage White's structure or to remove a defender of the center. More provocative.

**The cardinal rule for Black:** *Develop the light-squared bishop outside the pawn chain before playing ...e6.* Play `...e6` too early and you've buried your best piece — a beginner's mistake that turns a good Scandinavian into a bad French.

### 3.2 The ...c6 + ...e6 "Caro-shell" is Black's home base
A huge amount of Scandinavian middlegames feature Black pawns on **c6 and e6**, with the queen having retreated, the light bishop developed outside, knights on f6 and d7 (or b6/d5), and the dark-squared bishop on e7 or d6.

This "shell" is incredibly resilient. The c6-pawn covers d5 and b5; the e6-pawn covers d5 and f5. Together they form a small, sturdy box around Black's king. White cannot easily break it. **This is why you rarely lose the Scandinavian by being slowly outplayed** — the structure just holds.

### 3.3 The d5/d6 square and the c-file
In the `3...Qd6` lines especially, Black often plays `...c6`, and the d5-square can become an outpost for a White knight — but Black usually controls it well enough. Meanwhile the **half-open d-file (for Black after a future ...e5 or for White)** and **the c-file** are the main operating lanes. Know whether the position wants `...Rd8` or `...Rc8`/`...Re8`.

### 3.4 White's structural plans against the Scandinavian
White's typical setup in the main lines: `Nc3, d4, Nf3, Bc4 (or Be2/Bd3/g3-Bg2), Bf4 (or Bg5/Be3), Qe2, O-O-O (aggressive) or O-O (solid)`.

- **The kingside fianchetto + long castle** is a popular modern try for an edge (g3, Bg2, Bd2, O-O-O, then a pawn storm if Black has castled short).
- **The Bc4 + Qe2 + O-O-O** setup aims at a direct attack, especially against an early ...Qa5.
- **The quiet Be2/Bd3 + O-O** setup just develops soundly and relies on the small space edge.

### 3.5 The eternal tension: White's time vs Black's solidity
Keep this scoreboard in your head:
- **White's asset:** a tempo (or a pawn, in the gambits), more central space, the bishop pair sometimes.
- **Black's asset:** no weaknesses, a good light bishop, clear plans, and the long-term endgame structure.

If White attacks and it doesn't break through, Black is just *better* in the resulting calm position. So as Black you survive the early pressure and then take over. As White you must *create something concrete with your lead in development before it evaporates.*

### 3.6 The recurring tactical motifs (memorize these patterns)
- **Nb5 hitting c7/d6** — a constant White resource when Black's queen is on d6/a5 and c7 is loose. Black neutralizes with ...c6, ...Na6, or ...Qd8.
- **Bb5+/Bd3 hitting f5** — White trades or harasses the light bishop. Black often has ...c6 and ...Nbd7 ready.
- **Qe2 + O-O-O then d5 break or g4 push** — White's standard kingside expansion.
- **...Bb4 pinning the c3-knight** — Black's standard tempo-gaining developing move (especially in ...Qa5 lines), often combined with ...O-O-O.
- **The g4/Ne5 fork-trick traps** — see Part 5.

### 4.1 — Mainline `3...Qa5` (the Classical Workhorse)

```
1. e4 d5
2. exd5 Qxd5
3. Nc3 Qa5
```

**Why Qa5?** The queen sidesteps the tempo-attack to a square where it (a) is hard to hit again, (b) eyes the a5-e1 diagonal so a future ...Bb4 creates a pin, and (c) supports queenside expansion and queenside castling. It is the most natural and historically the most common retreat.

**The main tabiya continues:**
```
4. d4   Nf6
5. Nf3  c6      (the Caro-shell begins; covers b5 and d5)
6. Bc4  (or 6.Bd2, 6.Ne5, 6.g3 — all covered below)
```

#### The standard Black setup (memorize this picture)
Pawns: a7, b7, **c6**, **e6** (coming), f7, g7, h7
Pieces: Qa5, Bf5 or Bg4 (developed *before* ...e6!), Nf6, Nbd7, Be7, then ...O-O or ...O-O-O.

A model move order for Black:
```
6...Bf5   (bishop out first!)
7...e6
8...Bb4   (pin, gaining a tempo, eyeing the c3-knight)
8...Nbd7
9...O-O   (or ...O-O-O for opposite-side fireworks)
```

#### Answering the Five Questions in the 3...Qa5 mainline (as **Black**)
1. **What is each piece doing?**
   - *Light bishop (f5/g4):* your best piece — controls e4/the b1-h7 diagonal (f5) or pins/pressures f3 (g4). Out before ...e6.
   - *Qa5:* eyes b4/e1 diagonal, supports ...Bb4 pin and queenside play; ready to drop back to c7/d8 if harassed.
   - *Knight f6:* controls e4 and d5, primary kingside developer.
   - *Knight b8→d7:* flexible, supports ...e5/...c5 breaks and reroutes to b6 or f8.
   - *Dark bishop (b4/e7):* b4 pins c3 and gains tempo; e7 is the solid square.
   - *c6/e6 pawns:* the shell — cover b5, d5, f5.
2. **What is the pawn structure telling me?** Symmetrical-ish; Black is solid with no weaknesses. Black's freeing breaks are `...c5` (hitting d4) and `...e5` (central counter). White has slightly more central space (d4 vs ...e6) and will try `Ne5`, `Bf4`, and kingside expansion.
3. **Enemy weakness / my weakness?** White's weakness: the d4-pawn can become a target after ...c5; White's queenside can be loosened by the ...Bb4 pin. Black's weakness: the early queen sortie means *watch for Nb5/Bb5+ tempo shots and tactics on the a5-queen* (e.g. b4 ideas hitting Qa5 — see traps).
4. **What is the opponent threatening?** Common White threats: `Nb5` (hitting c7/a-stuff), `Bd2` then `Nb5`/discovered hits on the a5-queen, `Ne5` hitting f7/c6 and eyeing the bishop on g4. *Always check whether your Qa5 can be trapped or hit with tempo before you commit elsewhere.*
5. **Plan if nothing forcing:** Complete development (bishop out, ...e6, ...Bb4 or ...Be7, ...Nbd7, castle), then choose a break — `...c5` against White's center if White has castled long, or central solidity + piece play if White castled short. If opposite-side castling, race with `...b5-b4` and the open lines toward White's king.

#### Key sub-lines of 3...Qa5

**(A) 6.Bc4 — the aggressive bishop.**
```
6. Bc4 Bf5  7. Bd2 e6  8. Nd5!?  (a thematic jab)
```
After `8.Nd5`, the point is that `8...Qd8 9.Nxf6+ Qxf6` gives White a small lead in development and the bishop pair, but Black is fine and solid. Black can also consider `8...Qd8` lines or `7...c6` first to deny the Nd5 jump. **Black's antidote:** the move `...c6` (covering d5/b5) played early prevents most of White's tactical jumps. If you've already played ...c6, the Nd5 ideas lose much of their sting.

**(B) 6.Bd2 — preparing Nb5/queenside ideas and unpinning in advance.**
This quiet move prepares `Nb5` hitting c7 and unpins the c3-knight in advance so ...Bb4 is less annoying. Black continues normally: `6...Bf5 7.Bc4 e6 8.Qe2 Bb4 9.O-O-O Nbd7` and Black is comfortable, often castling queenside too, or short. Watch for `Nb5` — meet it with `...Na6` or `...Qd8` and `...Bb4` swaps.

**(C) 6.Ne5 — the central knight lunge.**
```
6. Ne5 c6  (or 6...Be6, 6...Nbd7)
```
White plants a knight on e5 hitting f7 and c6 and eyeing g4 (if Black went ...Bg4). **Black's antidote:** `...c6` and `...Nbd7` challenging the e5-knight; if White had hoped to fork with `Nxg4`-type tricks, having the bishop on f5 (not g4) sidesteps that. After trades on e5 Black is solid.

**(D) 6.g3 — the modern fianchetto (most testing).**
```
6. g3 Bf5  7. Bg2 e6  8. O-O Be7  9. Ne5 (or 9.h3, 9.Nd2)
```
White fianchettos, castles short (or sometimes prepares O-O-O), and presses on the long light diagonal and in the center. **Black's plan:** complete the shell (...c6 already in, ...e6, ...Be7, ...Nbd7, ...O-O), keep the f5-bishop healthy (meet h3/g4 expansion calmly, retreating ...Bg6 and being ready for ...h5 ideas), and look for the ...c5 break or piece play on the queenside. This is a long strategic game where Black's solidity shines.

> **3...Qa5 verdict:** rock-solid and the easiest to learn first. The one thing you must respect is *tactics against the exposed queen* (Part 5 traps). Otherwise you reach a clear, plan-based middlegame.

### 4.2 — `3...Qd6` (the Modern, Engine-Approved Line)

```
1. e4 d5
2. exd5 Qxd5
3. Nc3 Qd6
```

**Why Qd6?** This has become the most respected main line in modern practice. The queen on d6 is:
- **Flexible** — covers d5 and the d-file, supports both ...e5 and ...c5 breaks, and can swing to the kingside (g6/h6 ideas) or stay central.
- **Harder to harass than on a5** — it's not running into b4 tricks, and it controls more central squares.
- **Connected to a very specific, reliable setup** that engines love.

The main downside: the queen on d6 can be hit by `Nb5` (and the d6-square is in the knight's sights), so Black must always have `...c6`/`...Na6`/`...a6` resources ready.

#### The model Black setup (this is your bread-and-butter)
```
4. d4   Nf6
5. Nf3  a6!   (the modern key move — prevents Nb5 and prepares ...b5, ...Bb7/...Bf5)
6. ...  (White develops: Bc4, g3, Be2, Bd3, etc.)
```
Then Black aims for: `...g6` and `...Bg7` (a kingside fianchetto, very harmonious) **or** `...Bf5/...Bg4 + ...e6 + ...Be7`, plus `...Nbd7`, `...c6`, `...O-O`.

**The "...a6 + ...g6 + ...Bg7" plan** is especially clean: the bishop on g7 rakes the long dark diagonal, the king is safe after ...O-O, and the d6-queen and ...c6/...e6 shell hold the center. Many strong players use exactly this.

#### Two main Black formations after 3...Qd6:

**Formation 1 — The fianchetto (modern main line):**
```
3...Qd6 4.d4 Nf6 5.Nf3 a6 6.Bc4 (or 6.g3) b5 7.Bb3 Bb7
8...e6  9...Nbd7  ...g6? no — here keep it flexible; often ...Be7 and ...O-O
```
Actually the cleanest fianchetto route is: `5...a6 6.g3 (White) Bf5 (or ...g6) ...` — Black mirrors with ...g6/...Bg7. The double-fianchetto-ish structures are very solid.

**Formation 2 — The classical shell:**
```
3...Qd6 4.d4 Nf6 5.Nf3 a6 6.g3 Bg4 7.Bg2 Nc6 8.O-O O-O-O!?
```
Here Black even castles long and pressures d4 with ...Bg4 and ...Nc6 — sharp and double-edged, a favorite of aggressive Scandinavian players.

#### Answering the Five Questions in 3...Qd6 (as **Black**)
1. **Piece jobs:**
   - *Qd6:* central nerve-center — covers d5/d-file, eyes h2 (after ...Bf5 batteries in some lines), supports both pawn breaks. The key is it is *active but safe* once ...a6 stops Nb5.
   - *...a6:* small but vital — kills Nb5 and prepares ...b5 queenside space and ...Bb7.
   - *Light bishop:* f5/g4/b7 depending on plan — always developed actively.
   - *Dark bishop:* g7 (fianchetto plan) raking the long diagonal, or e7 (shell plan).
   - *Knights:* f6 (controls e4/d5), and b8→d7 or →c6 depending on whether you want the ...c5/...e5 break (d7) or direct pressure on d4 (c6).
2. **Pawn structure:** Black is solid; with ...b5 Black gains queenside space, with ...g6 Black gets a safe fianchetto. Breaks: `...e5` (the key freeing break — when it works Black equalizes fully and gets active piece play) and `...c5` (hitting d4). White has central space (d4) and may aim for c4/d5 clamps.
3. **Weaknesses:** Black's d6-queen square and the b6/c7 squares need an eye (Nb5/Bf4 ideas). White's d4-pawn and the c4-square (after ...b5/...c5) are Black's targets; an over-extended White queenside (after b-pawn pushes) can rebound.
4. **Opponent threats:** `Nb5` (if you haven't played ...a6!), `Bf4` hitting the d6-queen with tempo (be ready to step aside ...Qd8/...Qb4/...Qd7), `Ne5` central jumps. *Before any slow move, check: is my queen about to be hit by Bf4 or Nb5 with tempo?*
5. **Plan if nothing forcing:** finish the chosen formation (fianchetto or shell), castle, and prepare `...e5` — the thematic equalizing break. If you can't get ...e5 in, pressure d4 with pieces (...Nc6, ...Bg4) and/or expand with ...b5-...c5 on the queenside. In the ...O-O-O sharp lines, race on the queenside/center against White's king.

> **3...Qd6 verdict:** the best blend of solidity and flexibility, and the modern theoretical recommendation. Learn the `...a6` move and the `...e5` break and you have a complete, low-risk system. Slightly more nuanced than 3...Qa5 but more robust.

### 4.3 — `3...Qd8` (the Solid "Banker's" Retreat)

```
1. e4 d5
2. exd5 Qxd5
3. Nc3 Qd8
```

**Why Qd8?** The most modest retreat: the queen returns home, conceding that White gained a tempo but ensuring the queen is *completely safe* and out of all tactics. Black then sets up a super-solid shell and plays a slightly passive but extremely sturdy game.

This is the choice for players who want **zero** tactical risk and are happy to defend a slightly cramped but rock-hard position and outplay the opponent later.

#### Model Black setup
```
3...Qd8 4.d4 Nf6 5.Nf3 (or 5.Bc4) ...
Black: ...c6, ...Bf5 or ...Bg4 (out before ...e6!), ...e6, ...Be7, ...Nbd7, ...O-O.
```
Sometimes Black even goes for `...g6 + ...Bg7` here too. The defining feature is the passive-but-solid queen on d8 and a textbook Caro-shell.

#### Five Questions, 3...Qd8 (as **Black**)
1. **Piece jobs:** identical philosophy to the other lines — *light bishop out first (f5/g4)*, knights to f6 and d7, dark bishop to e7, pawns to c6/e6. The queen on d8 is temporarily passive but will come to c7 or b6 or support ...e5/...c5 later.
2. **Pawn structure:** the most purely "Caro-Kann-like" of the Scandinavian lines — extremely solid, no weaknesses, slightly less space. Break with `...c5` or `...e5` when ready.
3. **Weaknesses:** Black has essentially no weaknesses — that's the whole point — but also the least active piece (the d8-queen) and a bit less space. White's edge is purely spatial; convert your solidity into a good endgame.
4. **Opponent threats:** far fewer than in the queen-out lines, because the queen isn't a target. Mostly standard `Ne5`, `Bf4`, central pushes. Still: develop the bishop before ...e6, and watch `Ne5`/`Bxf7+`-type fork tricks if you've been careless.
5. **Plan if nothing forcing:** complete the shell, castle, and then slowly free yourself with `...c5` or `...e5`, aiming for a balanced middlegame or a pleasant equal endgame. Patience is the watchword.

> **3...Qd8 verdict:** the most solid and the lowest-theory of all. Slightly passive, but you will *never* get blown off the board. A great choice if you value safety and endgame play over early activity.

### 4.4 — `3...Qe5+?!` (the Offbeat One — Know the Refutation)

```
1. e4 d5
2. exd5 Qxd5
3. Nc3 Qe5+?!
```

**The idea:** a cheeky check that, after `4.Be2`, dreams of ...Qg5/...Bg4 tricks. **The problem:** it badly exposes the queen and loses time. You should know this mainly so you (as White) punish it and (as Black) avoid it.

**The refutation (as White):**
```
3...Qe5+ 4.Be2! (calmly blocks; threatens Nf3 hitting the queen)
...the queen has no good central home and White develops with tempo (Nf3, d4, O-O)
gaining a clear lead in development.
```
White just develops naturally — `Be2, Nf3 (hitting the queen again), d4, O-O, Bf4/Bg5` — and enjoys a tempo-rich, pleasant position. Black's queen will be chased around and White gets a near-free initiative.

> **Verdict:** don't play 3...Qe5+ as Black. As White, meet it with 4.Be2 and develop with tempo for a comfortable plus.

### 4.5 — The `2...Nf6` Modern Scandinavian (Knight Recapture Family)

```
1. e4 d5
2. exd5 Nf6
```

Black declines the immediate queen recapture and attacks d5 with the knight, intending to win the pawn back with a piece and avoid the early-queen-tempo issue entirely. White's three main tries:

#### (A) 3.d4 — the main line (let Black take on d5 with the knight)
```
2...Nf6 3.d4 Nxd5  (or the sharp 3...Bg4!? Portuguese — see 4.7)
```
After `3...Nxd5 4.c4 Nb6 (or 4...Nf6)`, White builds a big pawn center (c4+d4) and gains space; Black plays for piece pressure and the standard ...Bf5/...Bg4, ...e6, ...Be7, ...O-O, hitting the center with ...c5/...e5 later. White is slightly more comfortable (more space) but Black is sound. This often transposes toward Alekhine/Caro-Panov-like structures.

#### (B) 3.c4 — grab more space, defend the pawn
```
2...Nf6 3.c4 c6!?  (or 3...e6!? — the Icelandic Gambit, see 4.6)
```
With `3...c6`, Black hits d5 and after `4.dxc6 Nxc6` gets quick development and open lines for a pawn (a gambit feel), or White declines with `4.d4 cxd5 5.cxd5` reaching a Panov-like structure. Sharp and double-edged.

#### (C) 3.Nf3 — flexible development, often transposing
White just develops and may try to hold the d5-pawn or transpose. Black recaptures with ...Nxd5 at a good moment or strikes with ...Bg4 / ...c6.

#### Five Questions, 2...Nf6 Modern (as **Black**)
1. **Piece jobs:** the f6-knight is the star — it recaptures on d5 or pressures White's center; the light bishop still comes out to f5/g4 actively; Black develops quickly and fights for the initiative rather than just solidity.
2. **Pawn structure:** more fluid and gambit-flavored than the ...Qxd5 lines. If White builds c4+d4, Black targets it with ...c5/...e5/...e6 and piece pressure (the classic "hypermodern" plan of letting White build a center then attacking it).
3. **Weaknesses:** White's broad center can become over-extended (target with breaks); Black may be temporarily a pawn down in the gambit lines (compensated by development and open lines). Black's own setup is sound if the recapture is timed well.
4. **Opponent threats:** White trying to *keep* the extra pawn with awkward moves (...which often hands Black a big lead in development), or building an imposing center. *Watch for White's c4-d5 clamps and Bb5+ checks.*
5. **Plan if nothing forcing:** complete rapid development, regain the pawn at the right moment (or keep the initiative if gambiting), and break against White's center with ...c5/...e5. This family suits players who want activity and the initiative over the pure solidity of the queen lines.

> **2...Nf6 verdict:** sharper, more dynamic, more "fight for the initiative" than the queen recaptures. The gateway to the dangerous gambit lines below.

<a name="46"></a>
### 4.6 — The Icelandic Gambit `2...Nf6 3.c4 e6!?`

```
1. e4 d5
2. exd5 Nf6
3. c4 e6!?
4. dxe6 Bxe6
```

**The idea (Black is gambiting a pawn):** Black sacrifices the d-pawn to blast open the center and get a *huge* lead in development and the two bishops raking toward White's king. After `4.dxe6 Bxe6`, Black has the e6-bishop, will play ...Nc6, ...Bc5/...Bb4, ...O-O fast, and rip open lines while White is undeveloped.

**Why it's dangerous:** White is a pawn up but *cramped and behind in development*, and Black's pieces fly out with threats. Many White players get mated or lose material trying to hold the pawn.

**Black's typical continuation:**
```
4...Bxe6 5.Nf3 (or 5.d4) Nc6 6.d4 (or 6.Be2) Bb4+ / ...Bc5, ...Qe7, ...O-O-O
```
with raking bishops, pressure on c4 and the center, and fast attacking play. **As White**, the antidote is *calm, accurate development and timely return of the pawn* to defuse the initiative — don't be greedy.

#### Five Questions, Icelandic Gambit (as **Black**)
1. **Piece jobs:** the two bishops (e6 + dark-squared on b4/c5) are the engine of the attack; knights to c6 and f6 hit the center fast; rooks come to the open d- and e-files.
2. **Pawn structure:** Black is a pawn down but has open lines and a development lead — *this is dynamic compensation, not material parity.* Black plays fast or not at all.
3. **Weaknesses:** White's lagging development and the loose c4-pawn; if White survives the initiative, the extra pawn tells, so Black must press *immediately*.
4. **Opponent threats:** White consolidating (returning the pawn to develop) and reaching a safe, slightly-better endgame. Black must prevent calm consolidation.
5. **Plan if nothing forcing:** there *should* always be something forcing — that's the gambit's nature. Develop with threats, open lines, target the king and c4. If the attack fizzles, you're worse, so play energetically.

> **Icelandic verdict:** a fully sound, dangerous gambit and a great surprise weapon. High activity, real attacking chances, but you must play accurately and energetically.

---

<a name="47"></a>
### 4.7 — The Portuguese Gambit `2...Nf6 3.d4 Bg4!?`

```
1. e4 d5
2. exd5 Nf6
3. d4 Bg4!?
```

**The idea:** instead of recapturing on d5, Black develops the bishop to g4 with tempo (pinning/pressuring) and plans ...Qxd5 next, regaining the pawn with a great position — *or*, if White tries to hold the pawn, Black gets a raging initiative for it.

**Main line:**
```
3...Bg4 4.f3 (the critical test) Bf5 5.Bb5+ Nbd7 6.c4 e6! 7.dxe6 Bxe6
```
or `4.Be2 Bxe2 5.Qxe2 Qxd5` and Black has comfortably regained the pawn with easy development.

**Why it's dangerous:** if White grabs and tries to keep everything (`4.f3 Bf5 5.Bb5+ ... c4 ...`), Black sacrifices with ...e6 to open lines and gets a fierce attack with the bishop pair and leads in development, much like the Icelandic. If White plays modestly (`4.Be2` or `4.Nf3`), Black just equalizes cleanly by taking back on d5.

#### Five Questions, Portuguese Gambit (as **Black**)
1. **Piece jobs:** the g4/f5-bishop creates immediate pressure and provokes weakening White moves (f3); the queen recaptures d5 with tempo or supports the attack; rapid development and open lines are the goal.
2. **Pawn structure:** if Black regains the pawn (the calm lines), it's a healthy, equal structure with active pieces. In the sacrificial lines, dynamic compensation as in the Icelandic.
3. **Weaknesses:** White's f3 (if played) weakens the king's diagonal and e3; the d5-pawn is hard for White to hold. Black's only "weakness" is the temporary pawn deficit in the sharp lines, offset by activity.
4. **Opponent threats:** White consolidating with the extra pawn (in the sharp lines) — Black must keep generating threats; or White simply giving back the pawn for a calm equal game (fine for Black).
5. **Plan if nothing forcing:** regain the d5-pawn with the active ...Qxd5 setup, or if White overreaches to keep it, open lines with ...e6 and attack. Either way Black gets an easy, active game.

> **Portuguese verdict:** the most popular gambit try in the Modern Scandinavian — low-risk (you usually just regain the pawn with a great game) but with big upside if White is greedy.

<a name="48"></a>
### 4.8 — White's Try-For-An-Edge Setups (read this as **White**)

If you're playing **White** and want the best practical chances against the Scandinavian, here are the recommended weapons:

**Against 3...Qa5 / 3...Qd6 / 3...Qd8 (the queen lines):**
- **The g3 fianchetto system:** `Nc3, d4, Nf3, g3, Bg2, O-O (or Bd2 + O-O-O)`. You get the long light diagonal, central space, and a flexible setup. Against an early ...O-O by Black, consider `Bd2, O-O-O, h4-h5` storming. This is the modern engine-favored try for an edge and is hard for Black to fully neutralize.
- **The Bc4 + Qe2 + O-O-O attacking system** (especially vs ...Qa5): rapid development aimed at the f7/king, with `Nb5`/`Bf4` jabs at Black's queen. Sharp and dangerous if Black is inaccurate.
- **The quiet Bd3/Be2 + O-O** setup: just take the small space edge and play a normal game; very low-risk, slight pull.

**Against 2...Nf6 (the modern/gambit family):**
- **3.d4 Nxd5 4.c4 Nb6 5.Nf3** with a big center and space — the principled choice; don't be greedy with extra pawns in the Portuguese/Icelandic, *return material to complete development and consolidate.*
- **3.c4** trying to hold extra space, but be ready for the Icelandic (`3...e6`) — meet it with calm development (`Nf3, Be2, Nc3, O-O`) and a timely return of the pawn.

#### Five Questions for **White** (general, all lines)
1. **Piece jobs:** the c3-knight harasses Black's queen and supports d5/Nb5 jumps; the d4-pawn anchors central space; the light bishop (c4/g2/d3/e2) targets f7/the long diagonal; Bf4/Bg5 develops with tempo against Black's queen/knight; the queen (e2/d2) connects rooks and supports O-O-O.
2. **Pawn structure:** White has the central-space edge (d4 vs Black's ...e6/...c6). Your structural plan is to *maintain space and pry open lines* before Black's solid shell fully neutralizes you — consider d5 breaks, or c4-d5 clamps in the ...Nf6 lines.
3. **Weaknesses:** Black's early queen (in the ...Qxd5 lines) is your target — hit it with tempo (Nb5, Bf4) and gain development. Your own potential weakness: the d4-pawn (after Black's ...c5) and an over-extended kingside if your pawn storm doesn't connect.
4. **Black's threats to watch:** the freeing breaks `...c5` and `...e5` (especially ...e5 in the Qd6 lines — *prevent or prepare for it*), the ...Bb4 pin (in Qa5 lines), and in the gambits, Black's fast development and open lines — don't get greedy.
5. **Plan if nothing forcing:** complete development with tempo, choose your king's side (O-O-O for attack, O-O for solidity), and then either storm (h4-h5/g4 against a castled Black king) or clamp the center and exploit your space. *Convert your lead in development before it evaporates* — that's the entire White strategy.

<a name="part-5"></a>
## Part 5 — Traps & Gambits: The Trap Encyclopedia

This is the part that wins and loses games. Question 4 ("What is my opponent threatening *right now*?") lives here. Learn these cold.

### 5.1 — The classic ...Qa5 queen trap (BOTH sides must know this)

The exposed a5-queen can be **trapped** if Black plays carelessly. The general motif:

> When Black's queen is on a5 and Black plays a careless ...Bxc3-type capture or leaves the queen short of squares, White can sometimes play **b4** (hitting the queen) and follow with **Nd5/Bd2** ideas that cost Black the queen or material.

**As Black:** before any committal capture or pawn move, ask *"does b4 hit my queen, and do I have a flight square (c7, d8, b6, h5)?"* The safe recipe: play `...c6` (gives the queen the c7 retreat and covers b5/d5) and don't capture on c3 unless you've calculated the b4 resource.

**As White:** look for moments when the a5-queen is short of squares and `b4` (sometimes preceded by `Bd2`) wins material or the queen.

### 5.2 — The Nb5 fork/tempo shot (vs Qa5 and Qd6)

> White plays **Nb5**, hitting **c7** (fork of king/rook potential) and, with the queen on d6, hitting the **d6-queen** and **c7** simultaneously. If Black has not covered c7 (with ...c6, ...Na6, or ...Qd8), this wins material or forces awkward concessions.

**As Black:** the universal prophylaxis is **...a6** (in the Qd6 lines) or **...c6** (in most lines), which kills Nb5. In the Qd6 line, *play ...a6 early* — it's the whole reason the move exists.

**As White:** always check if Nb5 is a tempo shot or a fork before Black gets ...a6/...c6 in.

### 5.3 — The Bf4 "queen-hit" tempo (vs Qd6)

> With Black's queen on d6, **Bf4** develops *with tempo*, attacking the queen. Black must step aside (...Qd8, ...Qb4+, ...Qd7) — losing time — and White gains development.

**As Black:** be mentally prepared to move the queen; often `...Qd8` or `...Qb4+` (hitting something back) minimizes the time loss. This is *not* a disaster — it's a standard tempo White spends a developing move to gain. Just don't get tricked into a worse square.

**As White:** Bf4 (and sometimes Bg5 vs the f6-knight) is your standard tempo-gaining developer against the Scandinavian queen.

### 5.4 — The g4/Ne5 fork-trick against ...Bg4

> If Black develops **...Bg4** (instead of ...Bf5) and is careless, White can play **Ne5** (hitting the g4-bishop and f7/c6) or prepare **h3 + g4** to win the bishop pair or trap the bishop. A specific trick: after ...Bg4, White's **Ne5** can fork ideas if Black has loosened f7/c6.

**As Black:** if you play ...Bg4, be ready to meet `h3` with `...Bh5` (and `...Bg6` if `g4` comes, watching for ...h5 counter-tricks), or simply trade `...Bxf3` to damage White's structure. If you don't want the hassle, play **...Bf5** instead — it sidesteps all the Ne5/g4 tricks.

**As White:** harass an early ...Bg4 with h3/Ne5 to win the bishop pair or a tempo.

### 5.5 — The premature ...e6 punishment (a Black self-trap)

> If Black plays **...e6 *before* developing the light-squared bishop**, the bishop gets buried on c8 (a bad-French structure) and White gets a free positional advantage with no risk. This isn't a tactic — it's a strategic trap Black sets for *himself.*

**As Black:** the single most important move-order rule in the Scandinavian — **light bishop OUT (to f5 or g4) before ...e6.** Tattoo this on your brain.

### 5.6 — Greedy-pawn punishment in the gambits (a White self-trap)

> In the **Portuguese** and **Icelandic** gambits, if White grabs the d5/e6 pawn and then tries to *hold onto it* with awkward moves (early f3, Bb5+, c4 all to cling to material), White falls catastrophically behind in development and gets attacked/mated. The "trap" is greed.

**As White:** the antidote to both gambits is the same — **develop calmly and give the pawn back at the right moment** to neutralize Black's initiative and reach a safe (even slightly better) position. Material is not worth a lethal development deficit.

**As Black:** *invite* White's greed — keep posing concrete threats so that every greedy pawn-grab costs more development.

### 5.7 — The ...Qxd5 / Bxf7+ "fork trick" alertness (mostly vs careless Black)

> In some ...Nf6 lines where Black recaptures on d5 with the knight and is loose, White has **Bb5+** and **Bxf7+/Ne5** ideas hitting the king and forking. Also, leaving the king on e8 with an open e-file invites discovered ideas.

**As Black:** develop, don't leave the king in the center too long, and watch `Bb5+` checks — meet them with `...c6` or `...Nbd7` and resolve the center before White's pieces coordinate.

### 5.8 — The opposite-castling pawn-storm race (a strategic "trap" for the slow player)

> When White castles **O-O-O** and Black castles **O-O** (or vice versa), it becomes a *pawn-storm race* toward each other's kings. The "trap" is playing too slowly or defensively — whoever attacks faster and more accurately usually wins, and a single tempo can decide it.

**As Black (you castled short, White long):** counterattack with `...b5-b4` and open the b/c-files toward White's king; don't passively defend.
**As White (you castled long, Black short):** storm with `h4-h5`, `g4-g5` to open lines at Black's king; bring a rook to the h-file.

> **Trap-avoidance master rule (Question 4 in one line):** *Before every move, scan for checks, captures, and tempo-hits against my queen and king (Nb5, Bf4, b4, Bb5+, Ne5) — only then make my plan.*

<a name="part-6"></a>
## Part 6 — The Five Questions: Answer Bank by Color

A condensed, grab-it-mid-game reference. Find your line, read your color.

### ▶ As BLACK — the universal answers (true in almost every Scandinavian)

1. **What is each piece doing?**
   - *Light bishop (f5/g4):* your best piece, developed *outside* the chain — controls e4/the b1-h7 diagonal or pins/pressures f3.
   - *Queen (a5/d6/d8):* relocated to safety after the tempo-hit; supports breaks and (on a5/d6) queenside or central play.
   - *Knight f6:* controls e4/d5, main kingside developer.
   - *Knight d7 (from b8):* flexible, enables ...e5/...c5, reroutes to b6/f8.
   - *Dark bishop (e7/g7/b4):* e7 solid, g7 rakes the long diagonal, b4 pins c3 with tempo.
   - *c6 & e6 pawns:* the shell — cover b5, d5, f5; the source of your solidity.
2. **What is the pawn structure telling me?** You're solid with no weaknesses; you have slightly less central space. Your space/freedom comes from the breaks **...c5** (hits d4) and **...e5** (central counter, the key equalizer especially in Qd6). Aim a rook at whichever file your break opens (often the d- or c-file).
3. **Where is the enemy weakness / mine?** *Enemy:* White's d4-pawn (after ...c5), a loosened queenside (after the ...Bb4 pin), or an over-extended kingside pawn storm. *Mine:* the early-queen square (watch Nb5/Bf4/b4 tempo-hits) and slightly less space — neutralize by completing the shell and castling.
4. **What is my opponent threatening?** Scan for **Nb5** (cover with ...a6/...c6), **Bf4** (queen-hit on d6 — be ready to step aside), **b4** (queen-hit on a5 — keep a flight square), **Bb5+** (meet with ...c6/...Nbd7), and **Ne5/g4** tricks vs ...Bg4. 
5. **What's my plan if nothing forcing?** Finish the picture: *light bishop out → ...e6 → ...Be7/...Bg7/...Bb4 → ...Nbd7 → castle.* Then prepare **...e5** or **...c5**; or, in opposite-side castling, storm with **...b5-b4** toward White's king. You should never be planless: the structure dictates these breaks.

### ▶ As WHITE — the universal answers (true in almost every Scandinavian)

1. **What is each piece doing?**
   - *c3-knight:* gained the tempo on Black's queen; supports d5/Nb5 jumps.
   - *d4-pawn:* your central space anchor.
   - *Light bishop (c4/g2/d3/e2):* targets f7 and the long light diagonal; the spearhead of attacking setups.
   - *Bf4/Bg5:* develops *with tempo* against Black's queen/knight.
   - *Queen (e2/d2):* connects rooks, supports O-O-O and the central/kingside plans.
   - *Rooks:* to d1/e1 (or the storming file after O-O-O).
2. **What is the pawn structure telling me?** You have the **central-space edge** (d4). Your job is to *use your lead in development and space before Black's solid shell neutralizes it.* Look for the **d5** break or a **c4-d5 clamp** (in the ...Nf6 lines), or a kingside pawn storm after O-O-O.
3. **Where is the enemy weakness / mine?** *Enemy:* Black's early queen — hit it with tempo (Nb5, Bf4, b4) to gain development; Black's slight lack of space. *Mine:* the d4-pawn (after Black's ...c5), and an over-extended kingside if your storm doesn't connect; in the gambits, a development deficit if you get greedy.
4. **What is Black threatening?** The freeing breaks **...c5** and especially **...e5** (in the Qd6 lines — prevent or prepare for it), the **...Bb4** pin (Qa5 lines), and, in the gambits, fast development + open lines. *Don't get greedy with extra pawns in the Portuguese/Icelandic.*
5. **What's my plan if nothing forcing?** Develop with tempo (Nc3 already, then Bf4/Bc4/g3-Bg2, Qe2/Qd2), pick your king's side (**O-O-O to attack, O-O for solidity**), then **storm** (h4-h5/g4 vs a short-castled Black king) or **clamp the center** and squeeze your space edge. *Convert the development lead before it evaporates.*

## Part 7 — Plan Bank: What To Do From Move 10 Onward

You said the real fear is feeling clueless after move 10. Here is a concrete plan menu. Identify your structure, pick the matching plan, execute.

### BLACK plans (by situation)

**Plan B1 — The ...c5 break (vs a White center with d4).**
*When:* you've completed the shell and castled, White has a d4-pawn, and you want freedom/activity. *How:* prepare with ...Nbd7/...Nc6 and ...Rc8 or ...Rd8, then play ...c5. If White takes (dxc5) you recapture and get an open game with active pieces; if White pushes/ignores, you gain queenside space and pressure d4. *Goal:* dissolve White's space edge and activate your pieces.

**Plan B2 — The ...e5 break (the key equalizer, esp. Qd6 lines).**
*When:* you've developed harmoniously (often with ...Nbd7, ...Qd6/...Qc7, ...Be7) and White can't punish the center opening. *How:* prepare with ...Re8 and ...Nbd7, then ...e5. *Goal:* free your position completely, open lines for your active pieces and bishops; this is often full equality or better.

**Plan B3 — The queenside pawn storm (opposite-side castling; you're short, White's long).**
*When:* White has castled O-O-O. *How:* ...a5/...b5, then ...b4 hitting the c3-knight and prying open the b/c-files; bring a rook to b8 or c8; sac a pawn if it opens lines fast. *Goal:* mate or overwhelm White's king before White's kingside storm arrives. *Win the race.*

**Plan B4 — The fianchetto squeeze (...g6/...Bg7 setups, esp. Qd6).**
*When:* you've fianchettoed. *How:* the g7-bishop pressures d4 and the long diagonal; combine with ...c5 or ...e5 and ...Nc6/...Nbd7; double rooks on the half-open file your break creates. *Goal:* harmonious pressure on White's center and dark squares.

**Plan B5 — The gambit initiative (Portuguese/Icelandic).**
*When:* you've gambited a pawn. *How:* develop every piece with a threat, open the center (...e6 sac if needed), bishops raking the king, rooks to the open d/e-files, target c4 and f2/king. *Goal:* attack before White consolidates; if it fizzles, you're worse, so press relentlessly.

**Plan B6 — The solid endgame grind (3...Qd8, super-solid setups).**
*When:* the position has simplified and you're rock-solid but slightly cramped. *How:* free with ...c5/...e5, trade into a structure with no weaknesses, and outplay the opponent in a balanced or pleasant endgame. *Goal:* convert solidity into a long-game edge.

### WHITE plans (by situation)

**Plan W1 — The kingside pawn storm (you castled O-O-O, Black O-O).**
*How:* h4-h5 (hitting a ...g6 fianchetto or prying h7), g4-g5 (kicking the f6-knight), Rdg1/Rh-file, then sacrifice to open the king. *Goal:* mate Black's king; win the storm race.

**Plan W2 — The central clamp / d5 break.**
*How:* with a big center (c4+d4 in the ...Nf6 lines, or d4 in the queen lines), prepare and play d5 to gain space and open lines for your bishops, or clamp Black with c4-d5 and rooks on the d-file. *Goal:* convert your space edge into piece activity and a bind.

**Plan W3 — The tempo-harassment + lead-in-development squeeze.**
*How:* keep hitting Black's queen/awkward pieces with tempo (Nb5, Bf4, occasionally Bb5+), finish development first, seize the open files (d/e), and use your extra activity to win a pawn or infiltrate. *Goal:* turn the opening tempo into a concrete material or positional gain before Black equalizes.

**Plan W4 — The calm space-squeeze (you castled O-O).**
*How:* complete development (Bd3/Be2/Bg2, Bf4/Bg5, Re1/Rd1), keep the small central-space edge, restrain Black's ...c5/...e5 breaks (pieces on the right squares), and slowly improve. *Goal:* a risk-free, lasting pull.

**Plan W5 — The anti-gambit consolidation (vs Portuguese/Icelandic).**
*How:* develop minor pieces fast, castle, and *return the extra pawn* at the moment that kills Black's initiative; then enjoy a safe, equal-to-better position with the structure in your favor. *Goal:* defuse, consolidate, convert.

- `3.d4 Nxd5` — main, White gets space. (4.5)
- `3.d4 Bg4!?` — **Portuguese Gambit**, usually regains pawn with great game. (4.7)
- `3.c4 e6!?` — **Icelandic Gambit**, dynamic pawn sac for attack. (4.6)

4. *(Black)* In Qd6, play `...a6` to kill Nb5; in Qa5, keep a queen flight square vs b4.

8. *(Both)* Before every move, scan checks/captures/tempo-hits (Question 4) **then** plan.

**The Five Questions, always, at any move:**
1. What is each piece doing? 2. What is the pawn structure telling me? 3. Where is the enemy weakness and mine? 4. What is my opponent threatening right now? 5. What's my plan if nothing forcing?
