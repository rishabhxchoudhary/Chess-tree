# The Pirc Defence — A Complete Study

**1.e4 d6 2.d4 Nf6 3.Nc3 g6** (ECO B07–B09)

This guide is built so that at *any* point in a Pirc game you can answer the five questions:

1. **What is each piece trying to do?**
2. **What is the pawn structure telling me?**
3. **Where is the enemy weakness, and where is mine?**
4. **What is my opponent threatening right now?**
5. **What is my plan if nothing forcing is happening?**

You play *both* colours in this opening (you'll face it and you may adopt it), so every section gives you the plan for **White** and the plan for **Black**.

---

## How to read this guide

- Moves in **bold** are the mainline spine you should memorise.
- "①②③④⑤" tags answer the five questions in context.
- ⚠️ marks a trap or a move that loses/throws away the advantage.
- A *pawn break* is a pawn move that opens or resolves the central tension (the engine of every plan here).

---

## PART 0 — The Big Picture (read this first, it unlocks everything)

The Pirc is a **hypermodern** defence. Black deliberately lets White build a big pawn centre (pawns on d4 and e4, sometimes f4 and c4) and plans to **attack that centre later** with the pawn breaks **...e5** and **...c5**, backed by the fianchettoed bishop on g7.

**The single most important picture in the whole opening:**

> The g7-bishop is a sniper aimed down the long dark diagonal (g7–a1). Everything Black does is about **opening that diagonal**. Everything good White does is about **keeping it blocked** — usually by jamming a pawn on e5 or keeping the d4-pawn solidly defended.

This one idea answers question ① for Black's most important piece before the game even starts.

### The three things White can do (your entire map of the opening)

| White's choice | Move | Character | What White wants |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Austrian Attack** | 4.f4 | Sharpest, most critical | Build d4-e4-f4 roller, play e5, attack the king |
| **150 Attack / Byrne** | 4.Be3 / 4.Bg5 | Aggressive, easy to play | Qd2, Bh6 to trade the g7-bishop, castle long, pawn-storm with h4-h5 |
| **Classical (Two Knights)** | 4.Nf3 | Solid, positional | Develop naturally (Be2, 0-0), keep a small space edge, squeeze |

Black's *setup* (...Bg7, ...0-0, ...c6 or ...Nc6 or ...Na6, then ...e5 or ...c5) stays almost the same against everything. **That is the practical beauty of the Pirc**: you learn one shell and a handful of break-timing rules, not twenty forced lines. The hard part isn't the first 8 moves — it's knowing *which break to play and when*. That is exactly what Parts 1–4 drill.

### The King's Indian cousin

The Pirc structure is the King's Indian Defence **with White's c-pawn still on c2 instead of c4**. That single difference matters:

- White's c2-pawn means the c3-knight blocks White's c-pawn, so White's classic KID queenside expansion (c4–c5) is unavailable in the same way.
- It also means Black's ...d5 break can sometimes hit e4 *and* be harder for White to meet with a clamp, and White's centre is a touch less broad.
- **Takeaway:** if you know KID ideas, you already understand 70% of the Pirc. If you don't, the Pirc *teaches* you them.

# PART 1 — THE AUSTRIAN ATTACK (4.f4)

**1.e4 d6 2.d4 Nf6 3.Nc3 g6 4.f4 Bg7 5.Nf3 0-0** (the tabiya)

This is the critical main line and the one you must understand best. White has the dream centre: pawns on **d4, e4, f4**. The whole battle is whether that centre is a *steamroller* (White's dream) or *overextended and full of holes* (Black's dream).

```
White pawns: d4, e4, f4    — a broad, mobile, attacking mass
Black:       d6, g6 + Bg7  — coiled, waiting to strike with ...e5/...c5
```

### Reading the position (the five questions at the tabiya)

**① Piece jobs**
- *White's f3-knight*: defends d4, supports the e5-push, eyes g5/e5 outposts. It sits on f3 (not e2) precisely so the f-pawn could advance to f4.
- *White's c3-knight*: defends e4. This is its **one job and its weakness** — it is overworked, and ...c5/...d5 ideas or a pin with ...Bg4 (no, light squares) / pressure can exploit it. Note the recurring "the c3-knight is attacked twice" motif.
- *White's light bishop*: wants **d3** (eyeing h7 and supporting e5) or **c4/b3** (eyeing f7 and the a2–g8 diagonal). Where it goes defines the line.
- *Black's g7-bishop*: the sniper. Blocked by White's own e5/d4 pawns for now. Black's whole plan is to open it.
- *Black's f6-knight*: pressures e4. When White plays e5 it gets kicked and must find a good square (d5, d7, or h5 — **never** a bad retreat). Choosing that square well is half of Black's Austrian theory.

**② Pawn structure**
- White owns the centre and space; White's natural play is a **kingside/central pawn advance** (e5, then f5).
- Black is cramped but solid. Black's freeing breaks are **...e5** (hitting d4/f4) and **...c5** (hitting d4). Whichever break trades off White's centre opens the g7-bishop and equalises.
- White's f4-pawn slightly **weakens White's own king** (the e1–h4 diagonal and the g1–a7 diagonal get airy; ...Qb6+ and ...Ng4 ideas appear).

**③ Weaknesses**
- White: the **d4-pawn** (only the f3-knight and queen hold it), the **e5-square** once White commits f-pawn, and the king's cover after f4.
- Black: the **e6/d6 dark complex** and the **king** if White's e5–f5 storm lands before Black breaks.

**④ Threats right now (move 6)** — none forcing yet; it's a battle of *preparation*. But the looming threat is always **White e4-e5**, kicking the f6-knight and clamping the g7-bishop.

**⑤ Default plans**
- *White*: finish development (Bd3 or Bc4, 0-0 or Qe1-h4), then play **e5** and **f5** to storm the king.
- *Black*: arrange ...Nc6 or ...Na6 or ...c6+...Nbd7, then strike with **...e5** or **...c5** at the right moment to crack the centre.

---

## 1A. White's most aggressive try: 6.e5 (the immediate strike)

**5...0-0 6.e5** — White clamps immediately.

This is **critical but double-edged**. The point: kick the f6-knight and seize space before Black is ready.

**Main line: 6...dxe5 7.fxe5 Nd5 8.Bc4**

- ⚠️ **7.dxe5?** is the positional mistake — it opens the f8-bishop's diagonal and surrenders the centre. After 7...Qxd1+ 8.Kxd1 the queens come off and White's whole attacking point is gone. **White must recapture 7.fxe5** to keep queens on and keep the attack.
- After **8.Bc4**, Black has a choice on the d5-knight:
  - **8...Nxc3! 9.bxc3 c5** — the modern equaliser (championed by Pirc expert V. Onischuk). Black gets Grünfeld-style play against White's c3/d4/e5 pawn mass; the g7-bishop bites on the long diagonal and White's centre becomes a target. **This is Black's cleanest road to full equality.**
  - 8...Nb6 (frequent but suspicious — White's bishop retreats to b3 with pressure) and 8...Be6 (sharp, very theoretical) are the alternatives.

**Why 6.e5 doesn't fully work for White**: White's pieces aren't developed enough to support the advanced centre. The cheat-sheet line **6...dxe5 7.dxe5 Qxd1+** is the standard refutation of *premature* central advances — **pawn advances must be supported by pieces**, or they become targets. This is the single most important lesson of the Austrian Attack and it cuts both ways.

> **Trap for Black to know (the over-push):** 6.Bd3 Nc6 7.e5 dxe5 8.fxe5 Nh5 9.e6?! — White lunges. Black replies **9...f6!** and the over-extended e6/e5 pawns become weak rather than strong. Black is fine/better.

---

## 1B. White's solid main line: 6.Bd3 (the Weiss Variation)

**5...0-0 6.Bd3** — the most-played move. White finishes development first, *then* pushes e5 with support. This is the principled, best try.

Black has two serious setups. **Pick one and learn it.**

### Setup 1 (most popular): 6...Na6 — heading for ...c5

**6...Na6 7.0-0 c5 8.d5**

**① Piece jobs:** The a6-knight is *not* misplaced — it routes to **c7** (supporting ...b5 and ...Rb8) or supports ...c5. The g7-bishop waits for the centre to crack.
**② Structure:** After 8.d5 this becomes a **Benoni structure** — White has a protected passed-ish d5-pawn and central/kingside space; Black has the half-open c-file and a queenside pawn majority.
**③/⑤ Plans:** This is now a Benoni battle of flank vs. centre:
- *White* plays on the **centre and kingside** (e5 breaks, f5, piece play toward the king).
- *Black* plays on the **queenside**: ...b5 (often prepared by ...Rb8, ...a6, ...Nc7), rolling the pawns to open lines before White's kingside play arrives. **Black must generate queenside counterplay *before* White gets anything real on the kingside** — it's a race.

### Setup 2: 6...Nc6 — heading for ...e5

**6...Nc6** prepares **...e5**, hitting d4 directly. After **7.e5 dxe5 8.fxe5 Nh5** (the knight goes to h5, eyeing the f4/g3 squares and avoiding passivity). Black is fine with accurate play, but Setup 1 (...Na6/...c5) is considered to give Black more winning chances; 6...Nc6 was historically found to be a bit drawish/passive.

> **Practical recommendation (Black):** play **6...Na6** and aim for ...c5. It's the line with the most bite and a clear plan (queenside expansion) that you'll never feel lost in.

---

## 1C. Black's other main road: the Dragon Formation (5...c5)

**4.f4 Bg7 5.Nf3 c5** — Black strikes the centre *immediately*, before castling. Sharp and forcing. White's two tries:

### 6.Bb5+ — the tactical melee (know the forced draw!)

**6.Bb5+ Bd7 7.e5 Ng4 8.e6** — White lunges into Black's position.

Then the famous forcing sequence:

**8...fxe6 9.Ng5 Bxb5! 10.Nxe6 Bxd4! 11.Nxd8 Bf2+ 12.Kd2 Be3+** with **perpetual check / draw by repetition**.

- The point of **10...Bxd4!** is that Black *ignores* the attack on his queen, because after 11.Nxd8 the bishop check on f2 and e3 nets a perpetual. ⚠️ If you (as either colour) don't know this, you can blunder into a lost position or miss the draw.
- This line was thought bad for Black until **Seirawan played 8...fxe6 against Sax (1988)** and rehabilitated it.
- If White avoids the draw with **11.Nxb5**, the play is complicated and roughly balanced.
- **8...Bxb5** (instead of 8...fxe6) avoids the forced draw if Black wants to keep playing.

**Practical note:** Because 5...c5 6.Bb5+ gives White an *easy forced draw* (700+ database games end in this repetition), **many Black repertoire authors (e.g. Davies) avoid 5...c5 entirely** and recommend **5...0-0 6.Bd3 Na6** (Setup 1 above) to keep winning chances alive. Choose 5...c5 only if you're happy with a draw as Black, or you've studied 8...Bxb5.

White's other try is **6.dxc5** (allowing 6...Qa5, regaining the pawn with comfortable play — this is the Fischer–Spassky 1972 tabiya, see Part 6) or **6.Bb5+ Bd7 7.Bxd7+ Qxd7 8.d5**, which scores well for White but is not clearly justified theoretically.

### 6.e5 against 5...c5

**6.e5 Nfd7 7.exd6 0-0** is considered to offer Black good play — the d6-pawn that White won is weak and Black is fast in development.

---

## 1D. Austrian Attack — summary battle plan

**If you are WHITE:**
- Play **6.Bd3** (best), develop fully, castle, *then* break with **e5** and follow with **f5** to storm the king. Keep the g7-bishop blocked.
- Recapture on e5 with the **f-pawn** (fxe5), never the d-pawn, to keep queens on.
- Watch your own king — f4 created holes; beware ...Qb6+, ...Ng4, and tactics on the a7–g1 diagonal.
- ④ Black's threats to watch: a well-timed ...e5 or ...c5 that trades off your centre; ...Nxc3 wrecking your queenside; the ...b5 pawn-roll in Benoni structures.

**If you are BLACK:**
- Play **5...0-0 6.Bd3 Na6**, then **...c5**, then a queenside pawn-storm with **...b5** (after ...Rb8/...a6/...Nc7). You always have a plan: roll the queenside.
- Against 6.e5, equalise cleanly with **...dxe5 fxe5 Nd5** and **...Nxc3 + ...c5**.
- ④ White's threats to watch: **e5** clamping your bishop and kicking your knight (have the d5/h5 square ready); the **f5** storm against your king (break in the centre *before* it lands); White's bishop hitting f7 (from c4) or h7 (from d3).
- Your whole game is: **survive the space disadvantage, land a central break, unleash the g7-bishop.**

# PART 2 — THE 150 ATTACK & BYRNE (4.Be3 / 4.Bg5)

**1.e4 d6 2.d4 Nf6 3.Nc3 g6 4.Be3** (the 150 Attack — named because it was deemed simple enough for a 150-BCF / ~1800-FIDE player to beat titled players with).

This is, in practice, **the most dangerous line for an unprepared Black player**, because White's plan is brutally simple and the same every game.

### White's plan (memorise this — it's a recipe)

> **Be3 → Qd2 → f3 → 0-0-0 → Bh6 (trade the g7-bishop) → h4 → h5 → hxg6 (open the h-file) → Qh6 → mate ideas on h7/g7.**

**① Piece jobs (White):** Be3 and Qd2 line up to swap the g7-bishop via Bh6 — removing the defender of Black's dark squares and king. f3 props up e4 and prepares g4. The h-pawn is a battering ram to rip open the h-file for the rook + queen.
**② Structure:** Opposite-side castling (White long, Black short) → it's a **pawn-storm race**. Whoever's attack lands first wins.
**③ Weaknesses:** Black's kingside dark squares (esp. h7, g7, h6) once the g7-bishop is traded. White's king on c1 once Black gets ...b5-b4 going.
**⑤ Plan (White):** castle long, trade on h6, storm with h4-h5.

### Black's antidote — the key move-order rule

> **Against the Be3/Qd2 setup: DELAY castling, and counterattack on the queenside *first* with ...c6 and ...b5 (or ...a6 and ...b5).**

If Black robotically castles into it, White is simply faster:
⚠️ **4...Bg7 5.Qd2 0-0?! 6.0-0-0 c6 7.f3 b5 8.h4** and White's attack arrives first — this is the losing scenario the Pirc is infamous for.

**The fix — 4...c6 (or 4...a6):**

**4...c6 5.Qd2 b5** — Black gets the queenside rolling *before* committing the king. Now:
- **6.f3 Nbd7** (the standard tabiya, engine-approved ≈ +0.1, i.e. near-equal) and Black continues ...b4 hitting the c3-knight, ...a5, ...Qa5, ...Bb7, often *delaying or skipping kingside castling* so White's h-pawn storm has no target.
- A model line: **4...a6 5.Qd2 b5 6.f3 Nbd7 7.g4 Nb6 8.g5 Nfd7 9.h4 b4 10.Nd1 c5** — Black's queenside play is fast and White's kingside storm, with no Black king to hit yet, is far less dangerous. The space behind White's advanced pawns becomes a liability.

**Because of this**, White often *abandons* the long-castle plan against an early ...c6/...a6 and instead switches to:
- **A kingside (short) castle**, Classical-style, or
- An Austrian-flavoured **5.h3 Nbd7 6.f4** setup.

### The Byrne Variation (4.Bg5)

**4.Bg5** has the same Qd2-and-Bh6 idea **plus** the extra option of taking on f6 to wreck Black's pawns, and can transpose to an Austrian with f4 thrown in.

- **Black's antidote:** the bishop on g5 is *more vulnerable* than on e3. Hit it with **...h6** (and sometimes ...g5), forcing it to commit (Bh4) or retreat, which prevents the clean Bh6 trade and gains Black kingside space/tempo.
- A practical line: 4...Bg7 5.Qd2 h6 6.Bh4 g5 7.Bg3 Nh5 (grabbing the bishop pair / dark squares) 8.0-0-0 Nc6 with mutual chances.

### 150/Byrne — summary

**If you are WHITE:** Be3/Bg5 → Qd2 → f3 → 0-0-0 → Bh6 trade → h4-h5 storm. Simple and deadly *if Black castles early*. If Black delays castling and rolls the queenside, switch plans (castle short, or play f4).

**If you are BLACK:** **Do not castle into the storm.** Play **...c6 and ...b5** (or ...a6/...b5) first; trade or harass on the queenside; castle late or not at all. Against 4.Bg5, add **...h6** to disrupt the Bh6 plan. ④ The threat you're always watching: **Bh6 trading your best defender**, then **h4-h5-hxg6** opening the h-file.

# PART 3 — THE CLASSICAL / TWO KNIGHTS SYSTEM (4.Nf3)

**1.e4 d6 2.d4 Nf6 3.Nc3 g6 4.Nf3 Bg7 5.Be2 0-0 6.0-0**

White develops solidly and naturally — no pawn storm, just a **small, durable space advantage** and a plan to squeeze. Less critical than the Austrian, but you'll face it constantly because it's the "I don't want to study theory" choice for White. Black must avoid drifting into passivity.

**① Piece jobs:** White's pieces take natural squares (Nf3, Be2, 0-0, then Re1, often Be3/Bf4 and Qd2, h3). White's c3-knight again guards e4 and blocks the c-pawn. Black's g7-bishop and f6-knight do their usual jobs; Black adds pressure on **d4**.
**② Structure:** Classic centre vs. fianchetto. No immediate tension — Black *creates* it with ...e5 or ...c5.
**③ Weaknesses:** White's **d4-pawn** is the target (it's only held by the f3-knight + queen once pieces come off). Black's lack of space is the standing problem.
**⑤ Plans:**

### Black's two main setups

**Setup A — pressure d4 with pieces (King's-Indian-like):**
> **...Bg4 and ...Nc6**, hitting d4. After ...Bxf3 (removing d4's defender) and ...e5, Black gets KID-style play. The c3-knight (blocking White's c-pawn) actually *helps Black* here — White can't easily challenge with c4-c5 the way he would in a King's Indian.

**Setup B (recommended, solid) — the ...c6/...e5 break:**
> **6...c6 7.a4 Nbd7**, planning **...e5**. A model continuation: **6...e5 7.h3 c6 8.a4 exd4 9.Nxd4 Re8 10.0-0 Na6 11.Re1 Nb4** — note the two great knight squares **b4** and **e5**. The exchange ...exd4 opens the long diagonal for the g7-bishop, while White's pieces have no clear breakthrough. Grischuk held this comfortably vs. Caruana — proof it's sound at the top.

**Knight outposts to aim for (Black):** **e5** and **b4** are dream squares for Black's knights in this structure. Route them there (...Nbd7-e5 or ...Na6-b4).

### Classical — summary

**If you are WHITE:** develop naturally (Nf3, Be2, 0-0, h3, Re1, Be3/Bf4, Qd2), keep the centre intact, use your space to manoeuvre and *avoid* letting Black get a free ...e5/...c5. Your edge is small but nagging — play for a long squeeze, not a knockout.

**If you are BLACK:** don't sit passively. Pick a break: either ...Bg4/...Nc6 pressuring d4, or the solid ...c6 + ...e5. After the centre opens, get a knight to **e5 or b4** and activate the g7-bishop. Equality is very achievable here; your danger is *drift*, not tactics.

# PART 4 — RARER WHITE TRIES (don't get surprised)

| Line | Moves | What it wants | Black's reply |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Holmov / Bc4** | 4.Bc4 | Bishop eyes f7; sharp 5.Qe2 tries | 4...Bg7 5.Qe2 Nc6! leads to wild play (see trap below). Or simply develop and play ...e6/...Nc6 to blunt the bishop. |
| **Sveshnikov / Fianchetto** | 4.g3 | White fianchettos light bishop, positional | Black plays normally ...Bg7, ...0-0, ...c6/...e5; equalises with standard breaks. |
| **Bf4 (London-style)** | 4.Bf4 | Often via 1.d4 Nf6 2.Bf4 g6 3.Nc3 d6 4.e4 | ...Bg7, ...0-0, ...c6, ...Qb6 hitting b2/d4 is a good plan. |
| **150 via 3.f3 / KIA** | 3.f3 | Steers toward King's Indian setups (c4 coming) | ...g6 invites a KID; or ...e5 / ...d5 to avoid it. |

> **Trap in the 4.Bc4 line (know it as both colours):**
> 4.Bc4 Bg7 5.Qe2 Nc6 6.e5 — now **6...Ng4!** is Black's best: 7.e6 Nxd4 8.Qxg4 Nxc2+ avoids the heavily-analysed 6...Nxd4 7.exf6 Nxe2 8.fxg7 Rg8 9.Ngxe2 Rxg7, where White has *scored heavily in practice* even though theory calls it equal/unclear. The position is a queen-vs-three-minor-pieces imbalance — tread carefully.

# PART 5 — THE TRAP & THREAT CHECKLIST (answering Question 4 every move)

The Pirc punishes autopilot. Before every move, run this list. These are the concrete things that cause "trap-losses."

### When you are BLACK, watch for:

1. **e4-e5 clamps.** White's e5 kicks your f6-knight *and* shuts your g7-bishop. **Always have a good knight square ready (d5 or h5, occasionally d7).** A passive retreat is how Black gets slowly crushed.
2. **The f5 pawn-storm** (Austrian). If you haven't broken in the centre by the time White plays f5, your king is in danger. **Break with ...e5/...c5 *before* f5 lands.**
3. **Bh6 trading your g7-bishop** (150/Byrne). Losing this bishop = your king's dark squares collapse. Either prevent it (...h6 vs Bg5) or make sure you have queenside counterplay racing.
4. **Nd5 discovered/forking ideas.** In lines where your queen sits on d8 and White has a bishop on d2/g5 + knight on c3, **Nd5** can hit your queen with tempo. Prophylactic **...Qc7** or **...c6** defuses it. (This is exactly why ...Qc7 is "the optimal queen square" in many lines.)
5. **Castling into a storm.** Against Be3/Qd2/0-0-0, **delay ...0-0** and roll the queenside first.
6. **The 5...c5 6.Bb5+ forced draw.** If you want to *win* as Black, don't walk into the perpetual (8.e6 fxe6 9.Ng5 ... 12.Kd2 Be3+ =) unless you've prepared 8...Bxb5.
7. **Overextending your own ...g5/...h5.** Black's kingside pawn pushes (...h6, ...g5) are often *justified* in Byrne lines — but only when tactically supported. Loose pushes expose your own king.

### When you are WHITE, watch for:

1. **The wrong recapture: 7.dxe5?** in the Austrian. Recapture **7.fxe5** to keep queens on. dxe5 opens Black's bishop and trades queens — your attack evaporates.
2. **The over-push e6?!** (e.g. 9.e6 in the Bd3/Nc6 line). Black answers **...f6!** and your advanced pawns become weak. **Pawns need piece support.**
3. **...Nxc3 wrecking your queenside.** In 6.e5 lines, ...Nxc3 bxc3 gives Black a clean Grünfeld-style target on c3/d4. Don't assume your centre is strong — it can become a target.
4. **Your king after f4.** ...Qb6+ (hitting d4/b2), ...Ng4 (hitting e3/f2), and tactics on the a7–g1 diagonal all stem from the f4 weakness.
5. **Premature 6.e5 without development.** The whole reason 6.Bd3 is preferred over 6.e5 is that an advanced centre **without enough pieces behind it falls apart** (6...dxe5 7.dxe5 Qxd1+ is the standard refutation of the impatient push).
6. **Black's queenside roll in Benoni structures.** After d4-d5, ...b5 comes fast. If you dawdle on the kingside, Black's queenside attack lands first. It's a race — don't lose tempo.
7. **Letting Black get a knight to e5 or b4** in Classical lines. Those outposts neutralise your space edge. Prevent ...e5 / cover b4 (a3) when needed.

# PART 6 — A MODEL GAME TO ANCHOR EVERYTHING

**Spassky vs. Fischer, World Championship 1972, Game 17** — the only Pirc Fischer ever played in his career, and a perfect illustration of the Austrian Attack with ...c5.

> 1.e4 d6 2.d4 g6 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.f4 Bg7 5.Nf3 **c5** 6.dxc5 **Qa5** 7.Bd3 Qxc5 8.Qe2 0-0 9.Be3 Qa5 10.0-0 Bg4 11.Rad1 Nc6 12.Bc4 Nh5 13.Bb3 Bxc3 14.bxc3 Qxc3 ...

**What to learn from it:**
- **5...c5 6.dxc5 Qa5!** — Black regains the pawn (7...Qxc5) with an active queen and easy development. This is the calm alternative to the 6.Bb5+ melee.
- **13...Bxc3 14.bxc3 Qxc3** — Fischer gives up the prized g7-bishop to *grab White's weak c3/queenside pawns and damage the structure*. Lesson: the g7-bishop is precious, but **concrete pawn-grabbing and structural damage can be worth more than the bishop** when the centre is already resolved. Knowing *when* to trade your best piece is mastery.
- The game shows the **typical Austrian imbalance**: White's central/kingside space and bishop pair vs. Black's queenside pawn targets and active pieces. It was drawn — a fair verdict on the whole opening (White ≈ 39% wins, Black ≈ 28%, draws ≈ 33% in the master database; White is statistically a bit better but Black holds with accuracy).

# PART 7 — YOUR COMPLETE REPERTOIRE (the "what do I actually play" cheat sheet)

## As BLACK — one coherent, low-theory repertoire

1. **Setup (always):** ...d6, ...Nf6, ...g6, ...Bg7, then decide on castling based on White's plan.
2. **vs Austrian (4.f4):** 4...Bg7 5.Nf3 **0-0** 6.Bd3 **Na6**, then **...c5** and a queenside roll (...Rb8, ...a6, ...b5). Against 6.e5: ...dxe5 fxe5 **Nd5**, then **...Nxc3** and **...c5** to equalise.
3. **vs 150 (4.Be3):** **4...c6** (or 4...a6), then **...b5** — counterattack queenside, **delay castling**, don't walk into the h-pawn storm.
4. **vs Byrne (4.Bg5):** add **...h6** to harass the bishop and stop the clean Bh6 trade.
5. **vs Classical (4.Nf3):** 4...Bg7 5.Be2 0-0 6.0-0 **c6** and **...e5**; get a knight to **e5/b4**, open the g7-bishop.
6. **Golden rules:** (a) keep the g7-bishop unless you get concrete compensation; (b) time your central break *before* White's storm; (c) always know your f6-knight's square after e5; (d) play pieces first, pawn breaks second.

## As WHITE — pick your weapon

- **Want sharp, principled, best chance for advantage?** Play the **Austrian Attack**: 4.f4 Bg7 5.Nf3 0-0 **6.Bd3**, develop fully, then **e5** and **f5**. Recapture e5 with the **f-pawn**.
- **Want a simple, dangerous, low-theory plan?** Play the **150 Attack**: 4.Be3, Qd2, f3, **0-0-0**, **Bh6** trade, **h4-h5** storm. Just be ready to switch to short castling if Black plays an early ...c6/...b5.
- **Want a quiet positional squeeze?** Play the **Classical**: 4.Nf3, Be2, 0-0, h3, Re1 — keep the centre, deny Black his breaks, grind the space edge.
- **Golden rules:** (a) don't over-push pawns without pieces behind them; (b) keep the g7-bishop blocked (e5 pawn); (c) in opposite-castling races, don't lose a tempo — it's a sprint; (d) mind the holes f4 creates around your own king.

# PART 8 — THE FIVE QUESTIONS, AS A PERMANENT WORKFLOW

Tape this to your monitor. Run it every move once you're out of book.

1. **What is each piece doing?** → Is my g7-bishop (Black) / centre (White) doing its job? Is any piece *overworked* (White's c3-knight is the classic)? Is any piece passive and in need of a better square (Black's f6-knight after e5)?
2. **What is the pawn structure saying?** → Where's my space (the side my pawns point)? **Which break (...e5, ...c5, ...b5 for Black; e5, f5, h4-h5 for White) opens lines *in my favour*?** Push *there*; defend where you're cramped.
3. **Where are the weaknesses?** → Black: aim at d4, the e5-square, c3 after ...Nxc3. White: aim at Black's dark squares (esp. after trading the g7-bishop) and the cramped, slightly loose Black king.
4. **What is my opponent threatening *right now*?** → Run the Part 5 checklist. Most Pirc losses are e5-clamps, f5/h-pawn storms landing too early, the Bh6 trade, or an Nd5 tactic on an undefended queen. Spot it, then prevent or out-race it.
5. **What's my plan if nothing forcing is happening?** → Black: prepare and play your break, then activate the bishop / get a knight to e5 or b4 / roll the queenside. White: complete development, keep the bishop blocked, advance your storm pawns on the side you have space — *but only with pieces supporting them*.

## One-paragraph summary you can memorise

> The Pirc is a King's-Indian-without-c4: Black concedes the centre and lives to blow it up with **...e5/...c5**, unleashing the **g7-bishop**. White picks one of three plans — **f4 (Austrian)** to storm with e5/f5, **Be3/Qd2/0-0-0 (150)** to trade the g7-bishop and h-pawn-storm, or **Nf3/Be2 (Classical)** to squeeze. Black's universal answers: castle and roll the queenside vs. the Austrian (6...Na6, ...c5, ...b5); **delay castling and counter-roll the queenside** vs. the 150; break with ...c6/...e5 vs. the Classical. The recurring trap-losses come from White over-pushing pawns with no pieces behind them, or Black getting clamped by e5 and stormed before breaking. Keep the g7-bishop, time your break, watch the e5-square, and you'll never be clueless after move 10.
