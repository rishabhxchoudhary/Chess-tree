# The King's Fianchetto Opening (1.g3) — A Complete Study Guide
### Understanding the plan, not memorizing the moves

---

**Who this guide is for.** You are rated around 800. You have played the White side of 1.g3 and also faced it as Black, and in both cases you reached the middlegame with no idea what you wanted. This guide fixes exactly that.

**The core promise:** at every stage of the game you will be able to answer the five questions you asked — what each piece is doing, what the pawns are telling you, where the weaknesses are, what your opponent threatens right now, and what your plan is when nothing forcing is happening.

**How to read this.** Notation in 20 seconds: files are letters a–h (left to right from White's side); ranks are numbers 1–8. So g3 means the g-file, 3rd rank. N = knight, B = bishop, R = rook, Q = queen, K = king; a pawn move is just the square. "x" = capture, "+" = check, "O-O" = castling kingside. Read this with a real or online board in front of you (lichess.org "Analysis board" is free) and play the moves as you read — you'll remember ten times more.

## Part 1 — The Big Idea (read this first)

Most openings start with a pawn punch in the center — `1.e4` or `1.d4`. The King's Fianchetto Opening does the opposite. With `1.g3` White says: "I am not going to grab the center with pawns yet. Instead I am going to point a bishop *at* the center from the side, finish developing safely, and only then decide how to attack."

That bishop is the whole point. After `1.g3` and `2.Bg2`, the bishop sits on g2 and stares down the long diagonal a8–h1 — straight through the middle of the board. This is called a **fianchetto** (Italian, roughly "little flank"). Everything White does in this opening is built around that bishop and the squares it controls.

> ### The single most important fact about 1.g3
>
> **1.g3 almost always turns into the King's Indian Attack (the "KIA").**
>
> White's plan is a **system**: the same handful of moves work against almost anything Black does. The standard setup is:
>
> `Nf3, g3, Bg2, O-O, d3, Nbd2, e4` — and usually later `Re1` and the big central push `e5`.
>
> Because it is a system, **you do not need to memorize twenty different lines.** You need to understand **one plan** and the handful of ways Black can try to disturb it. That is the entire reason this opening is perfect for a player who hates feeling lost on move 10.

There is one more thing the name hides. "King's Indian Attack" means White is voluntarily playing the setup that Black normally uses in the **King's Indian Defense** — but with the colors reversed, so White gets it with an **extra move**. An extra move, called a *tempo*, matters a lot. It means White is usually the one attacking, and Black is usually the one trying to hold and counterpunch.

### What White wants in one sentence

> **White:** build a safe house on the kingside, clamp the center with `e4–e5`, and then throw the kingside pawns and pieces at Black's king (the `h4–h5` pawn storm plus the knight maneuver `Nf1–Nh2–Ng4`).
>
> **Black:** strike back on the **queenside** — the side where Black has more room — with `...b5, ...a5, ...b4, ...a4`, trying to rip open lines around White's queenside before White's kingside attack lands. It is a race.

Hold that picture in your head — **White attacks the kingside, Black attacks the queenside** — because it explains almost every move both sides make. When you are unsure what to do, the honest default is: "Am I White? Push toward the enemy king. Am I Black? Blow open the queenside."

## Part 2 — The Job of Every Piece

This is the heart of question 1 ("what is each piece trying to do?"). Below is the full White setup, piece by piece. For each one: where it goes, **why** it goes there, and what it is **not** allowed to forget.

### The light-squared bishop (g2) — the soul of the opening

- **Job:** control the long light diagonal a8–h1, x-raying through the center toward Black's queenside and the b7/c6/d5 squares.
- **Why g2 and not c4 or d3?** On g2 the bishop is safe behind its own pawns, it is almost impossible to attack, and it supports the central break `e4`. It is a long-term piece — it may not do anything flashy for fifteen moves, then suddenly decide the game when the center opens.
- **Do not forget:** this bishop's power switches on the moment the center opens. Trading off Black's light-squared bishop, or opening the long diagonal with `e4`, is often worth more than grabbing a pawn. Never block it with your own pawn on e4 permanently — that is why White plays `d3` and not `d4` early.

### The king's knight (f3) — the calm anchor

- **Job:** control the central squares e5 and d4, support the `e4–e5` advance, and guard White's king after castling.
- **Why f3?** It is the single best developing square for this knight in almost every opening — it hits the center and helps the king get safe.
- **Later life:** once the pawn reaches e5, this knight often defends e5 and can leap to g5 to attack Black's king when the time comes.

### The queen's knight (b1 → d2 → f1 → h2/e3 → g4) — the traveler

- **Job:** this is the attacking knight, and it goes on a long journey. The route is the most important maneuver in the whole opening: `Nb1–d2–f1–h2–g4`.
- **Why this strange path?** On d2 the knight supports `e4`. But d2 is a modest square. So once the center is set, the knight reroutes: to `f1` (regrouping), then `h2`, then the juicy `g4`, where it attacks f6 and h6 — right next to Black's king. From f1 it can alternatively go to `e3` to hit d5 and f5.
- **Plain-language version:** "My queenside knight isn't doing much where it is, so I walk it over to the kingside to help mug the enemy king." If you remember only one maneuver from this guide, remember `Nd2–f1–h2–g4`.

### The dark-squared bishop (c1 → f4 or g5) — the late hero

- **Job:** it stays home early (White plays g3/Bg2 first), then develops to `f4` — where it defends the key e5 pawn and eyes Black's kingside — or to `g5` to pin or trade.
- **Why so late?** White's first job is king safety and the center. This bishop has no good early square (its own pawns on d3/g3 are in the way), so it waits until `e5` has been played and then comes out with purpose.
- **Attacking role:** in the final assault this bishop sometimes sacrifices itself on h6 to tear open Black's king. That is advanced, but know the idea exists.

### The rooks (f1 → e1, and a1) and the queen (d1 → e2/e1)

- **f1-rook → e1:** after castling, the f-rook swings to e1. Why e1? It backs up the `e4–e5` push and the e-file, and it frees f1 for the traveling knight. This little move (`Re1`) is a quiet signpost that the e5 break is coming.
- **a1-rook:** often stays home to defend the queenside (especially the a2 pawn) while Black attacks there. Sometimes it lifts via a different route, but its first duty is defense on the side where Black is throwing pawns.
- **Queen → e2 or e1:** the queen supports e5 from e2, or swings via e1–h4/g3 to join the kingside attack. In the Fischer model game it lands on h5/h6 to help deliver mate.

### The king (e1 → g1) — get it safe, fast

- **Job:** castle kingside as early as possible (usually by move 5 or 6) and then **get out of the way**. A safe king is what lets White attack with a clear conscience.
- **Reminder:** the pawn went to g3, so the bishop on g2 is now part of the king's shelter. Do not carelessly trade that bishop off while your king lives behind it — the light squares around your king (f3, h3, g2) can become drafty.

### Quick-reference: the White army at a glance

| Piece | Goes to | Its one job |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| **Bg2** | g2 (stays) | Rake the long diagonal; switch on when the center opens |
| **Nf3** | f3 (stays) | Hold e5/d4; guard the king; jump to g5 later |
| **Nd2** | d2 → f1 → h2 → g4 | Travel to the kingside to attack the enemy king |
| **Bc1** | f4 (or g5) | Defend e5; join the kingside attack |
| **Rf1** | e1 | Support e4–e5; free f1 for the knight |
| **Ra1** | a1 (stays) | Defend the queenside against Black's pawn storm |
| **Qd1** | e2 / e1 → h4 | Support e5, then reinforce the attack |
| **Ke1** | g1 (castle) | Get safe early so the attack can fly |

## Part 3 — What the Pawns Are Telling You

This answers question 2 (space and pawn breaks) and question 3 (weaknesses). Pawns are the skeleton of the position. In the KIA the skeleton is very recognizable, and once you can read it, you always know which way to play.

### The defining structure: White's e5 wedge

The position White is aiming for has a White pawn on **e5** and **d3**, with Black pawns typically on **d5, e6** and a c-pawn on c5. Read the wedge:

- **White pawn on e5** is a spike jammed into Black's position. It does two things: it takes the f6 square away from Black's knight (so that knight can't sit in front of the king defending it), and it gives White more room on the kingside to bring pieces over.
- **Space = the side your pawns point.** White's pawns (e5) crest toward the kingside, so White has more room there → White attacks there. Black's pawns (c5, b-pawn, a-pawn) crest toward the queenside, so Black has more room there → Black attacks there.
- **This is the whole game in one picture:** a diagonal line splits the board. White owns the kingside half, Black owns the queenside half. Each side attacks where they are bigger.

### The pawn breaks — the levers that open the position

A **pawn break** is a pawn push that attacks an enemy pawn and threatens to open a file or diagonal. Breaks are how closed positions get cracked open. Knowing **your** break vs. the **enemy's** break is the difference between a plan and aimless shuffling.

| Side | The break | What it opens / achieves |
|------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **White** | `e4–e5` | The signature push. Gains kingside space, kicks the f6-knight, starts the attack. |
| **White** | `h4–h5` | The storm. Pries open the h-file and the squares around Black's castled king. |
| **White** | `f4–f5` | Sometimes added to the storm to blast open lines toward e6/g6. |
| **Black** | `...b5–b4` | Black's main break. Attacks White's c3/queenside, opens lines for Black's rooks. |
| **Black** | `...a5–a4` | Supports ...b4 and cramps White's queenside (can fix a2 as a target). |
| **Black** | `...c5–c4` | Sometimes used to rip open the queenside at the base of White's pawns. |

### Where are the weaknesses? (question 3)

- **Black's built-in problem — the "bad" light-squared bishop.** In the most common lines Black plays ...e6, which locks Black's own light-squared bishop (the c8-bishop) behind its pawns on d5 and e6. That bishop can spend the whole game doing nothing. This is Black's single biggest structural headache, and it is exactly why the KIA scores well: Black is effectively playing with one piece tied behind their back.
- **White's built-in soft spot — the e5 pawn and the d4 hole.** White's proud e5 pawn cannot be defended by another pawn (the d-pawn is on d3, the f-pawn often hasn't advanced). So Black piles up on it — with ...Qc7, ...Nd7, etc. — to try to win or trade it. The square d4 in front of White's d3-pawn can also become a home for a Black knight if White is careless.
- **Black's self-inflicted risk — the open queenside.** When Black throws ...b5–b4 and ...a5–a4 forward, those pawns can also become weak and the Black king (still on g8) relies on its own kingside pawns staying put. If White's storm arrives first, Black's king is the one in danger.

### A special case: when Black plays ...d6 and ...e5 instead

If Black sets up with pawns on **d6 and e5** (a King's-Indian-Defense-style fianchetto, often after ...g6 and ...Bg7), then White **cannot** play the thematic `e5` because Black's pawn already controls that square. White must switch plans: prepare `d4` to build a center, or play on the queenside with `c3 and b4`. Recognizing this early saves you from pushing into a wall.

## Part 4 — The Main Lines, Explained Move by Move

Now we put it together. Because 1.g3 is a system, the move **order** is flexible — White is just assembling the same setup. Below are the lines you will actually meet, grouped by what Black does. For each, you get the moves, then the "why" behind every White move, then the middlegame plan.

> **The move-order point (don't panic about transpositions).** You may reach the same position via `1.g3`, via `1.Nf3`, or via `1.e4 e6 2.d3`. They funnel into the same structures. Focus on the **target setup** — `Nf3, g3, Bg2, O-O, d3, Nbd2, e4` — and just steer toward it. If Black does something unusual, keep developing toward that picture and you will rarely go wrong.

### Line A — Black plays ...d5 and ...e6 (the most common, and most important)

**Moves:** `1.g3 d5  2.Bg2 Nf6  3.Nf3 e6  4.O-O Be7  5.d3 O-O  6.Nbd2 c5  7.e4 Nc6  8.Re1 b5  9.e5 Nd7`

**Why each White move:**

1. `1.g3` — clear the way for the bishop to reach g2.
2. `2.Bg2` — the fianchetto. The bishop now eyes the center and d5.
3. `3.Nf3` — develop, control e5, prepare to castle.
4. `4.O-O` — king to safety before anything else can go wrong.
5. `5.d3` — modest but vital: it supports a future e4 without blocking the g2-bishop (which `d4` would).
6. `6.Nbd2` — the traveling knight begins its life on d2, backing up e4.
7. `7.e4` — White finally contests the center, and the g2-bishop's diagonal starts to breathe.
8. `8.Re1` — rook behind the e-pawn; quietly prepares e5 and frees f1 for the knight.
9. `9.e5` — the signature push. The knight on f6 is kicked to d7, White seizes kingside space, and the attack begins.

> #### The five questions, answered at move 9 (this position)
>
> **1. What is each piece doing?** g2-bishop rakes the long diagonal; f3-knight and e5-pawn form the central clamp; d2-knight is about to travel (f1–h2–g4); e1-rook supports e5; the c1-bishop will come to f4.
>
> **2. What do the pawns say?** White's e5 points kingside → White attacks the kingside. Black's b5/c5 point queenside → Black attacks the queenside.
>
> **3. Weaknesses?** Black's c8-bishop is bad (stuck behind e6/d5). White's e5-pawn needs babysitting. Mine to watch: the queenside light squares once Black pushes ...b4.
>
> **4. What does Black threaten right now?** ...b4 next, hitting White's queenside and trying to open lines. Not an immediate mate — but the start of Black's counter-race.
>
> **5. My plan if nothing forcing happens?** Bf4, then Qe2, then h4 and the knight tour Nf1–h2–g4, then h5 — march at the Black king.

**How the attack continues (the model plan).** From here White's ideal sequence is roughly: `Nf1`, `h4`, `Bf4`, `N1h2`, `Ng4`, `h5`, and then break with `h6` or sacrifice on f6/h6 to expose the king. Meanwhile Black races with `...b4, ...a5, ...a4, ...Ba6, ...Qb6`. Whoever's attack lands first usually wins. White's is often faster because Black's light-squared bishop is so passive.

### Line B — The same thing via 1.e4 (vs a French- or Sicilian-style setup)

If you start with `1.e4` and Black answers `1...e6` (French) or `1...c5` (Sicilian), you can still steer into the KIA:

**Example:** `1.e4 e6  2.d3 d5  3.Nd2 Nf6  4.Ngf3 c5  5.g3 Nc6  6.Bg2 Be7  7.O-O O-O  8.Re1`

This is the exact same structure as Line A. The only difference is move order. Note `3.Nd2` — White plays the knight to d2 first specifically to avoid an early queen trade after ...dxe4. Everything else is identical: castle, `Re1`, `e5`, attack. If you understand Line A, you already understand this.

### Line C — Black copies you (the ...g6 / ...Bg7 fianchetto)

Some opponents mirror White: `1.g3 g6  2.Bg2 Bg7  3.Nf3 d6  4.O-O e5`. Now Black's pawn on e5 stops your `e5` push cold. Do not fight the wall.

- **Don't force e5 — it isn't available.** Instead aim for `c3 and d4` to build your own center, or expand on the queenside.
- **A nice practical try:** meet the symmetrical fianchetto with `3.b3` and an opposing fianchetto (Bb2), which carries some tactical sting on the long dark diagonal.
- **Mindset:** when your main break is blocked, your job is to find the *other* break (here, d4) rather than shuffling aimlessly. That awareness is exactly what stops the "lost on move 10" feeling.

### Line D — Black plays ...c6 and pins with ...Bg4 (the "Yugoslav")

After `1.Nf3 d5  2.g3 Nf6  3.Bg2 c6  4.O-O Bg4`, Black develops the light-squared bishop **outside** the pawn chain to g4 (solving the "bad bishop" problem) and pins your f3-knight. This is one of Black's more sensible setups.

Play `d3`, `Nbd2`, `h3` (asking the bishop's intentions), and aim for `e4` in the center as usual. If Black takes on f3, you recapture and your bishop pair plus the half-open file give you a comfortable game. The point: even Black's best setups give White an easy, plan-rich position. You are never worse out of the opening.

## Part 5 — Traps and How Not to Lose to Them

This is question 4 ("what is my opponent threatening right now?") made concrete. Most beginner losses are not deep strategy failures — they are one-move blunders or falling into a known trick. Here are the ones that actually happen in 1.g3 / KIA positions, for **both colors**.

### Trap 1 — The greedy pawn grab on e4 (you win material as White)

**Position:** a line where Black has a bishop on b7 and the center has just been traded, e.g. `1.Nf3 Nf6  2.g3 b6  3.Bg2 Bb7  4.O-O e6  5.d3 d5  6.Nbd2 Be7  7.e4 dxe4  8.dxe4`. Black greedily grabs: `8...Nxe4??`

**The punishment:** `9.Ne5!` — suddenly Black's knight on e4 is pinned against the loose bishop on b7 along the long diagonal, and f7 is in trouble. After the forced `9...Nd6` (the only move), `10.Bxb7 Nxb7  11.Qf3` double-attacks f7 and the stranded knight on b7. White wins material.

**Lesson for both sides:** the g2-bishop and the f3-knight form a hidden tactical net on the long diagonal. As Black, never snatch the e4-pawn while your own bishop sits undefended on b7. As White, always check whether ...Nxe4 walks into Ne5.

### Trap 2 — The fianchetto-bishop fork/skewer on the long diagonal

Because the g2-bishop looks straight down a8–h1, any Black piece that lands on that diagonal **undefended** — especially the queen, a rook on a8, or a knight on c6 — can be won by a discovered attack when White moves the f3-knight (e.g. Nf3–e5 or Nf3–d4 unveiling the bishop).

- **Practical rule (White):** before you commit to an attack, look whether a knight jump uncovers your bishop onto something valuable. These "free" tactics are how the KIA wins many games.
- **Practical rule (Black):** keep the long diagonal respected. Don't leave your queen or rook on a8/b7/c6 lined up with that bishop without a defender.

### Trap 3 — Pushing the kingside storm too early (a White self-trap)

The `h4–h5` storm is thrilling, but if you launch it **before** your king is castled and your center is set, you can open lines toward your **own** king.

**Rule:** first `O-O`, first `e5`, first `Re1/Bf4`. Only when the house is built do you light the fuse with `h4`. Patience is part of the system — the attack is stronger when it's prepared.

### Trap 4 — As Black, getting mated by the h-pawn (the Fischer treatment)

This is the nightmare you probably lived through when you faced 1.g3 as Black. White clamps with e5, you drift on the queenside without enough urgency, and White's `h4–h5–h6` plus `Ng4` and `Qh5` crashes through. The defense is in Part 6 — but the one-line summary:

- **Be fast on the queenside.** It's a race. Don't make slow, tidy moves; push ...b5–b4 with purpose.
- **Don't weaken your kingside for free.** A careless ...h6 or ...g6 can be the exact hook White's h-pawn wants to smash. Keep your king's pawns healthy unless you've calculated.
- **Trade off White's attackers.** If you can swap the light-squared bishops or the traveling knight, White's attack loses its teeth.

> ### A universal anti-trap habit (do this every single move)
>
> **Before you move, ask: "If I pass, what would my opponent play, and does it hurt me?"** This one question — checking the enemy's reply — prevents the overwhelming majority of blunders at the 800 level. It directly answers your question 4, every move, for the rest of your chess life.

## Part 6 — Playing AGAINST 1.g3 (the Black side)

You told me you had no idea how to play this as Black. Good news: Black is completely fine against 1.g3 — White's extra-tempo system is comfortable but not dangerous **if you know the plan**. Here is a clear, low-theory way to meet it.

### Black's simplest healthy setup

**Recommended moves:** `1.g3 d5  2.Bg2 Nf6  3.Nf3 c6  4.O-O Bg4!`  (or ...Bf5)

- **...d5 grabs the center** — you take the space White declined to take.
- **...c6 supports d5** and gives your queen the c7/b6 squares and your bishop the route back if needed.
- **...Bg4 (or ...Bf5) is the key.** It develops your light-squared bishop **outside** the pawn chain — solving the one structural problem that plagues Black in the main lines. This is the single most important idea for you: **get your light-squared bishop out before you play ...e6.** If you play ...e6 first and trap it on c8, you've volunteered for the bad position the KIA is designed to give you.

### Black's five questions, answered

> **1. Piece jobs:** ...Bg4/Bf5 = your good bishop, active outside the chain; ...Nf6 and ...Nbd7 (or ...Nc6) control e5 and e4; your dark-squared bishop comes to e7 or d6; you castle and get safe.
>
> **2. Pawns:** you have a healthy d5/c6/e6 center. If White plays e4 you can take (...dxe4) to open the position for your now-active pieces.
>
> **3. Weaknesses:** once your light bishop is outside the chain, you have no real weaknesses. White's d3 is slightly passive and the d4-square can be yours.
>
> **4. What White threatens:** the e4–e5 clamp and the h4 storm — but only if you let White set up unopposed. Contest the center early and the storm never gets going.
>
> **5. Your plan if nothing forcing:** complete development, challenge the center with ...e5 or ...dxe4 at the right moment, and look for queenside space with ...b5 — or simply reach a balanced, comfortable middlegame.

### The golden rule for Black

> **Develop your light-squared bishop to g4 or f5 BEFORE locking it in with ...e6.** If you remember nothing else as Black, remember this. It is the antidote to everything unpleasant about facing the King's Indian Attack.

## Part 7 — The Model Game (see the plan win)

One well-understood game teaches more than a hundred memorized moves. The most famous KIA game ever is **Bobby Fischer vs. Myagmarsuren, Sousse 1967**. Play it on a board. Watch the system you just learned produce a checkmating attack out of a quiet opening.

**Fischer – Myagmarsuren, Sousse Interzonal 1967**

```
1.e4 e6 2.d3 d5 3.Nd2 Nf6 4.g3 c5 5.Bg2 Nc6 6.Ngf3 Be7 7.O-O O-O
8.e5 Nd7 9.Re1 b5 10.Nf1 b4 11.h4 a5 12.Bf4 a4 13.a3 bxa3 14.bxa3 Na5
15.Ne3 Ba6 16.Bh3 d4 17.Nf1 Nb6 18.Ng5 Nd5 19.Bd2 Bxg5 20.Bxg5 Qd7
21.Qh5 Rfc8 22.Nd2 Nc3 23.Bf6 Qe8 24.Ne4 g6 25.Qg5 Nxe4 26.Rxe4 c4
27.h5 cxd3 28.Rh4 Ra7 29.Bg2 dxc2 30.Qh6 Qf8 31.Qxh7+ 1–0
```

**Black resigned:** after 31.Qxh7+ Kxh7 32.hxg6+ (double check and the rook on h4 mates) it is forced mate. White's entire plan was exactly the system from this guide.

**What to notice as you play through it:**

- **Moves 1–9:** Fischer just builds the standard setup — Bg2, O-O, d3, Nd2, e4 — and clamps with `8.e5`. No fireworks yet.
- **Moves 10–16:** the two races run side by side. Black hurls `...b5–b4, ...a5–a4` at the queenside; Fischer reroutes knights (`Nf1, Ne3`) and starts the storm with `11.h4`.
- **Move 13.a3!:** Fischer's famous improvement — he calmly defuses part of Black's queenside push before turning to the attack. Defense and attack in one move.
- **Moves 17–25:** the traveling knight reaches `g5`, the queen swings to `h5`, the dark bishop lands on `f6` — every attacker Fischer owns converges on the Black king.
- **Moves 26–31:** the `h-pawn` storms in (`27.h5`), lines crack open, and `Qxh7+` finishes. Black's queenside counterplay arrived one move too late — the classic KIA story.

**The single takeaway:** Fischer never "calculated a brilliant combination" out of nowhere. He followed the **system** — safe king, central clamp, knight tour, pawn storm — and the combination *appeared* because his pieces were all pointed at the enemy king. That is what playing with a plan looks like.

## Part 8 — Your Anti-Cluelessness Checklist

Keep this next to you while you play. After the opening — and on every quiet move — run through the five questions. This is the exact tool that stops the "I have no idea what I want" feeling on move 10.

> ### The five questions — ask them every quiet move
>
> **1. What is each of my pieces doing?** Is any piece doing nothing? (In this opening: is my d2-knight still asleep? Send it on the tour. Is my c1-bishop home? Bring it to f4.)
>
> **2. What are the pawns telling me?** Which way do my pawns point? Attack that side. What is my pawn break (White: e5/h5; Black: b4/a4)? Prepare it.
>
> **3. Where are the weaknesses?** His: the bad c8-bishop, the squares around his king. Mine: my e5-pawn, my queenside light squares. Defend mine, target his.
>
> **4. What does my opponent threaten right now?** Pretend it's his move. What would he play? If it's a real threat, deal with it before anything else. (This prevents the blunders in Part 5.)
>
> **5. What's my plan if nothing is forcing?** White: Bf4, Re1, h4, Nf1–h2–g4, h5 → mate the king. Black: ...b5–b4, ...a5–a4, open the queenside. Always have the next quiet improving move ready.

### White's move-by-move skeleton (memorize the order)

| Phase | Do this | Because |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Opening | `g3, Bg2, Nf3, O-O, d3, Nbd2, e4` | Build the safe house and the center. |
| Clamp | `e5` | Gain space, kick the f6-knight, start the attack. |
| Regroup | `Re1, Bf4, Qe2` | Support e5; aim pieces at the kingside. |
| Storm | `h4, Nf1–h2–g4, h5` | Open lines at the enemy king. |
| Break in | `h6, or sac on f6/h6` | Expose the king and mate. |

### Black's move-by-move skeleton

| Phase | Do this | Because |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Opening | `d5, Nf6, c6, Bg4/Bf5` | Take the center and free the light bishop FIRST. |
| Develop | `e6, Be7/Bd6, Nbd7, O-O` | Finish development with no bad pieces. |
| Contest | `...dxe4 or ...e5` | Challenge White's clamp before it forms. |
| Counter | `...b5, ...a5, ...b4, ...a4` | Win the race on the queenside. |

## Part 9 — Mini-Glossary & How to Practice

### Words used in this guide

| Term | Plain meaning |
|------|---------------|
| Fianchetto | Developing a bishop to g2/b2 (or g7/b7) behind a pawn, aiming it down a long diagonal. |
| KIA | King's Indian Attack — the system 1.g3 almost always becomes. |
| Tempo | A single move's worth of time. Having an extra tempo means you're effectively one move ahead. |
| Pawn break | A pawn push that hits an enemy pawn to open a file or diagonal. |
| Pawn chain / wedge | A diagonal line of pawns; the e5-pawn is White's wedge into Black's position. |
| Clamp / bind | A pawn (like e5) that cramps the opponent and takes key squares away. |
| Bad bishop | A bishop stuck behind its own pawns with little scope — Black's c8-bishop in the main line. |
| Transposition | Reaching the same position by a different move order. |
| Pawn storm | Marching pawns (h4–h5–h6) at the enemy king to rip the position open. |

### How to actually get good at this (a 4-week plan)

1. **Week 1 — Play the setup on autopilot.** Play 1.g3 in fast online games. Just reach Nf3/Bg2/O-O/d3/Nbd2/e4 every time. Don't worry about results; build the muscle memory.
2. **Week 2 — Add the clamp and the tour.** Now consciously play e5 when allowed, then the knight tour Nf1–h2–g4 and h4–h5. Notice when the attack works and when it doesn't.
3. **Week 3 — Run the checklist.** In every game, on every quiet move, literally ask the five questions from Part 8. Slow down. This is where the real improvement happens.
4. **Week 4 — Review and play the Black side.** Re-play Fischer–Myagmarsuren. Then play the Black defense from Part 6 (get that light bishop out early) so you're never lost on either side again.
